Ethics & Underlying Philosophies

Society · Morality · Ethics · 6 Ethical Theories · Historical Philosophers

Core Concepts
Society

A large group of people who live together in an organized way, sharing work and making decisions following rules/laws. Rules aim to make life better for everyone over time.

Morality

A society's rule of conduct — principles describing what people ought and ought not to do: what is right/wrong, good/bad, fair/unfair. Encompasses values and norms/practices. Shaped by culture, religion, upbringing, or personal values.

Ethics (from Greek "ethos" = custom, character)

The philosophical study of morality. Involves rational examination (systematically analyzing right/wrong using reason and logic) and evaluation of people's behaviour (judging actions to determine if morally right/wrong). Also used as a set of professional guidelines (Code of Conduct).

Moral Values Examples: Honesty, Justice, Respect, Integrity, Compassion

Norms/Practices Examples: Punctuality, Greetings, Politeness, Queuing

Key Distinctions:
• Moral vs Ethical: Moral = good/bad of behaviour; Ethics = examines behaviour to decide if good/bad
• Unethical vs Illegal: Illegal = against law; Unethical = against principles (may or may not be illegal)
• Ethics vs Etiquette: Ethics = standards of conduct; Etiquette = polite manners (e.g. business card exchange)

⬡ The Six Ethical Theories

Ethical theories serve as frameworks for moral decision-making. Given the same scenario, different theories may lead to different conclusions. Always structure your answer as: identify action → apply theory → reach verdict.

1. Relativism
NOT Workable

No universal moral norms. One person says "X is right," another says "X is wrong" — both can be correct. What is right/wrong depends on context and perspective.

✓ Pros

Respects diverse perspectives; avoids cultural arrogance; allows "agree to disagree"

✗ Cons

No clear answers; can justify harmful behaviour; no universal standards in globalized world

Subjective Relativism: Each person decides right/wrong for themselves. "What's right for you may not be right for me." Case: abortion, corporal punishment

Cultural Relativism: Right/wrong depends on a society's actual moral guidelines. Varies from place to place and time to time. Case: arranged marriage, handshake greetings

2. Ethical Egoism
NOT Workable

Morality is guided entirely by self-interest. An action is morally right if it benefits the individual performing it. Each person should focus exclusively on their own happiness/well-being.

✓ Pros

Practical — aligned with natural inclination; promotes self-reliance; can benefit community (entrepreneurs creating jobs)

✗ Cons

Encourages selfishness; can harm others; leads to conflicts; not practical for society-wide cooperation

Example: Mark exaggerates his contributions to get a promotion — justified under egoism because it maximizes his self-interest

3. Divine Command Theory
NOT Workable (for persuasion)

Good actions = those aligned with God's will. Bad actions = those contrary to God's will. Holy books serve as moral decision-making guides. "God is all-good and all-knowing."

✓ Pros

We owe obedience to our Creator; God is the ultimate moral authority; clear moral rules

✗ Cons

Different holy books may disagree; doesn't work in multicultural societies; doesn't address all modern problems; doesn't work for non-believers

4. Kantianism ★ HIGH EXAM PRIORITY
WORKABLE ✓

Founded by Immanuel Kant. Actions guided by universal moral laws derived through reason, not personal feelings or consequences. Focus on action and duty, NOT outcome.

Key Concepts:

Good Will: Doing the right thing because it is right — not for personal gain, rewards, or fear

Dutifulness: Actions done out of moral duty, irrespective of outcomes

Categorical Imperative (CI): An unconditional rule that always applies

1st Formulation — Universalizability: "Act only from moral rules that you can at the same time will to be universal moral laws." If a rule self-destructs when universalized → it is WRONG.

2nd Formulation — Humanity as an End: "Act so that you treat both yourself and other people as ends in themselves and never only as a means to an end." Never use people as mere tools.

✓ Pros

Rational reasoning; universal moral guidelines; treats all persons as moral equals

✗ Cons

Sometimes no single rule covers an action; can't resolve conflicts between moral rules

5. Utilitarianism ★ HIGH EXAM PRIORITY
WORKABLE ✓

Developed by Jeremy Bentham & John Stuart Mill. Principle of Utility (Greatest Happiness Principle): An action is right if it leads to the greatest happiness for the greatest number. Focus on consequences/outcomes, not intent.

Act Utilitarianism: Evaluates each individual action based on whether it produces the greatest happiness. Uses cost-benefit analysis. Happiness (positive) vs. Unhappiness (negative). If benefits exceed harms → action is good.

Rule Utilitarianism: A rule is right if, when followed by everyone, it leads to the greatest happiness for the most people. Avoids the problem of "moral luck."

Key difference from Kantianism: Utilitarianism focuses on CONSEQUENCES; Kantianism focuses on DUTY/RULES regardless of outcome

✓ Pros

Focuses on happiness; practical; comprehensive in weighing consequences

✗ Cons

Hard to predict all consequences; ignores duty/individual rights; tangible vs. intangible values hard to compare

6. Social Contract Theory
WORKABLE ✓

Society functions based on agreed-upon rules/laws that people follow for mutual benefit. Based on ideas by Rousseau, Hobbes, and James Rachels.

Hobbes: People need strong government to enforce moral rules; the social contract = establishment of rules + a body to enforce them

Rousseau: Laws should reflect what is best for everyone; no one above the law

Negative Rights (Absolute): Others must leave you alone to exercise your right (e.g. free expression)

Positive Rights (Limited): Others must take action to help you access this right (e.g. free education)

John Rawls: Justice as fairness — equal basic rights for all; inequalities only justified if they benefit the least advantaged (Difference Principle)

✓ Pros

Focuses on rights; explains selfish behavior without rules; explains relationship between people and government

✗ Cons

No one literally signed the contract; can't resolve conflicting rights; may be unfair to those incapable of fulfilling their side

Historical Philosophers

Socrates

Greek philosopher. Famous for the dialectic method — exploring ideas through structured dialogue, thesis vs antithesis, and probing questions. Chose to face an unjust death rather than escape (theme of obeying law). Public opinion is NOT important; what matters is truth and justice.

Plato

Student of Socrates, teacher to Aristotle. Founder of the Academy in Athens. Wrote dialogues (Socrates often a character). Key work: Crito — dialogue where Socrates argues for obeying the law even when facing unjust death penalty. Theme: Obedience to Law.

Issues Pertaining to Network Communication

Spam · Censorship · Identity Theft · Cyberbullying · Internet Addiction

📧 Email and Spam ▼
Spam Definition

The mass distribution of unsolicited messages over the internet. Primary goal: force messages on recipients who did not opt to receive them. Used for advertising, scams, phishing, or spreading malware.

Distribution channels: Email (49%), Phone calls (26.5%), SMS (14.7%), Messaging apps (9.8%)

Key Attributes of Spam: (1) Anonymity — sender identity concealed; (2) Mass mailing — large quantities; (3) Unsolicited — recipient didn't consent

Types of Spam Content:

  • Commercial Advertising — promotions sent without consent
  • Fraudulent Schemes — lottery fraud, get-rich-quick
  • Phishing Attacks — fake websites/login forms to steal data
  • Malware Distribution — links that install harmful software

How spammers get emails: Opt-in lists (shared/sold), Dictionary Attacks (automated email generation), Data Breaches

CAN-SPAM Act 2003 (USA): Controlling the Assault of Non-Solicited Pornography And Marketing Act. Effective Jan 1, 2004. 3 email categories: (1) Transactional — must have accurate sender info; (2) Commercial — must have opt-out + postal address; (3) Unsolicited — must include "This is an advertisement" notice. 2024 update: fines up to $51,744 per email.

Criminal Offenses under CAN-SPAM: Hijacked computers, False IP addresses, Disguised sources, Falsified headers, Fraudulent accounts → can lead to IMPRISONMENT

Malaysia: No specific anti-spam law, but: CMA 1998 s.233(1)(b) — if used to harass/annoy (RM50,000 or 1yr jail); PDPA — illegal to use personal data without permission (RM500,000 or 3yrs); Penal Code s.507A — repeated unwanted messages = stalking

🔒 Censorship ▼
Definition

The control or suppression of information, speech, or media content. Can be enforced by governments, organizations, or individuals.

Types:

  • Direct Censorship: (1) Government monopolization; (2) Prepublication review; (3) Licensing & registration
  • Self-Censorship: Individuals voluntarily refrain from publishing — to avoid harassment, maintain government relations, avoid controversy

Why Internet Censorship is Challenging: Many-to-many communications; dynamic content; vast number of websites; cross-border jurisdiction issues; age verification difficulty

Ethical Views:

  • Kant's Opposition: Censorship prevents free thinking; violates autonomy
  • Mill's Opposition: Suppressing opinions stops people from learning truth; all voices must be heard

Child Internet Protection Act (CIPA) — USA: Requires schools/libraries to filter explicit content. Upheld by Supreme Court. Kantian critique: Violates autonomy. Act Utilitarian: Depends on benefit-harm weighing. Social Contract: Restricts individual choice.

🔐 Trust Issues, Identity Theft & Cyberbullying ▼

Identity Theft: Unauthorized use of another person's electronic identity, often for financial fraud. Leading forms: Credit Card Fraud and Phishing Scams. Prevention: strong passwords, MFA, monitor statements regularly

Online Predators: Individuals exploiting others online, especially minors. Tactics: Grooming (building trust to exploit), Catfishing (fictional persona), Blackmail & Coercion

Cyberbullying: Using digital platforms to intimidate, harass, or humiliate — to inflict psychological harm. Common forms: Doxxing (sharing private info), Trolling (deliberately upsetting posts), Threats & Blackmail

Sexting: Sending sexually suggestive messages/images. Legal risk: may be classified as child pornography for minors. Social consequences: reputation damage, emotional distress, cyberbullying.

Internet Addiction: Compulsive behaviour despite negative consequences. Test by Kimberly Young — 5+ "yes" out of 8 questions may indicate addiction. Signs: neglecting responsibilities, losing track of time, irritability when offline.

Intellectual Property, Privacy & Malaysian Cyber Laws

Copyright · Patent · Trademark · Trade Secret · Fair Use · PDPA · Privacy

Intellectual Property (IP) — STAR TOPIC ★
Definition

Any original idea or creation that has commercial value and comes from the mind. Includes human knowledge and ideas (intangible assets). Examples: books, songs, movies, paintings, inventions, computer programs.

IP issues involve: who owns the creation and whether others can copy or use it. Law gives creators exclusive rights for a limited time — then it enters the public domain.

1. Copyright

Protects: Original creative works (books, songs, software, movies, art, broadcasts)

Duration: Lifetime + 50–70 years after author's death (varies by country)

Automatic? YES — protection begins when work is created, no registration needed

Malaysia: Copyright Act 1987

Rights given: Make copies, sell/share, show/perform in public, create derivative works

If employee creates work during their job → copyright belongs to EMPLOYER

2. Patent

Protects: Inventions (machines, systems, new ideas/processes)

Duration: 20 years from filing date

Automatic? NO — must apply and be approved (via MyIPO in Malaysia)

Malaysia: Patent Act 1983

Rejected if: idea already known, differences too small/obvious, invention not useful

Patent owner can license others to use the invention for revenue

3. Trademark

Protects: Signs representing a product/service (words, logos, images, sounds, colors)

Duration: Does NOT expire as long as actively used and renewed

Symbols: ® (registered), ™ (unregistered)

Generic risk: If brand name becomes a common word (e.g. aspirin, yo-yo, thermos, escalator), trademark can be lost

Case: McDonald's vs McCurry — McDonald's lost; customers could distinguish between the two

4. Trade Secret

Protects: Private business information providing competitive advantage

Duration: Indefinite — as long as it stays secret

Registration: Not required

Examples: Coca-Cola recipe, manufacturing processes, customer lists, business strategies

Lost when: Reverse engineering (legal), employee movement/transition

Protection: NDA, confidentiality agreements, limited access, encryption

Fair Use — STAR TOPIC ★

Sometimes you can use copyrighted material without permission if the purpose is educational, informative, or non-commercial. Only applies to copyrighted works, NOT patents or trademarks.

FactorFavors Fair UseAgainst Fair Use
1. Purpose & CharacterEducational, critical, research, transformative useCommercial purposes
2. Nature of WorkNon-fiction/factual; published worksFiction; unpublished works
3. Amount UsedBrief excerptsEntire chapters or whole works
4. Effect on MarketDoesn't hurt original's sales; out-of-printDisplaces purchase of original
Software Copyright & Piracy

Software Piracy: Unauthorized duplication or use of computer software.

Types of Software Piracy:

TypeDescription
Soft liftingUsing one licensed copy on multiple computers (sharing with friends/co-workers)
Uploading/DownloadingMaking unauthorized copies available online for free download
Software CounterfeitingSelling illegally duplicated software that looks genuine
RentingUnauthorized renting of software for temporary use
Hard Disk LoadingPre-installing unauthorized software on computers to attract buyers
OEM UnbundlingSelling bundled software separately from the intended hardware

Proprietary Software: Owned by individual/company. Source code not public (trade secret). Users need license. Examples: Microsoft Windows, Adobe Photoshop.

Open-Source Software: Source code shared publicly. Anyone can view, use, modify, improve. Usually free. Examples: Firefox, Linux, VLC, WordPress, Blender.

Public Domain & Creative Commons

Works enter public domain via: Copyright Expiration, Failure to Renew, Voluntary Release (Dedication), or No Copyright Protection from the start.

Creative Commons (CC) Licenses:

  • CC BY (Attribution): Can use, share, adapt (even commercially) — MUST give credit to creator. Cannot claim work as your own.
  • CC0 (Creative Commons Zero): Creator gives up copyright entirely → public domain. No need for permission or attribution (though good practice). Can use freely, even commercially.
Privacy & PDPA — STAR TOPIC ★
Privacy

Having control over your personal life and information — what to share, with whom, and for what purpose. Violating privacy is an insult to the person's dignity.

Information Privacy

Protecting your personal data — how it is collected, stored, shared, or used. Privacy dilemma: sharing data is necessary for modern services, but you may lose control once shared.

Classification of Information:

TypeDescriptionExamples
Personal InformationPrivate, identifies you directly or indirectly; remains private unless you choose to discloseIC/passport number, phone, home address, health status, bank account
Public InformationOnce personal but now accessible to others (with/without consent)Health records shared with insurer, browsing cookies, Login with Google, social media posts
Public RecordCreated and published by official bodiesBirth certs, marriage certs, criminal records, LHDN records

Types of Disclosure: Voluntary (filling a form), Involuntary (CCTV, online tracking), Statutory (required by law)

Solove's Taxonomy of Privacy Violations
StageViolation TypeExample
1. Information CollectionCollecting data without knowledge/consentWebsite tracking clicks without telling you
2. Information ProcessingUsing data in unexpected/unagreed waysCompany profiling, data mining without consent
3. Information DisseminationSharing/spreading data without permissionPersonal photos posted online without consent

PDPA 2010 (Personal Data Protection Act) — CRITICAL ★

Laws of Malaysia Act 709. Date of Royal Assent: 2 June 2010. Effective: 10 June 2010. Regulates the processing of personal data in commercial transactions. Unlike most countries, Malaysia has NO specific Privacy Act — only PDPA for commercial data.

5 Rights Granted to Individuals under PDPA:

#RightWhat It Means
1Right to Access Personal DataSee what information a company has about you — ensures transparency
2Right to Correct Personal DataAsk for wrong/outdated info to be corrected — maintains accuracy and fairness
3Right to Withdraw ConsentTake back permission for company to use your personal data
4Right to Prevent Processing Causing DamageStop company from using data if it causes harm, embarrassment, or emotional stress
5Right to Prevent Processing for Direct MarketingRefuse companies from using your data for advertising/promotional messages

Data User: An entity (individual or organization) that collects, uses, processes, or stores personal data; decides how and why personal data is processed. Violation of PDPA by selling data without consent → penalties + loss of trust

Key Ethical Principles for Information Privacy: Autonomy & Control · Confidentiality · Informed Consent · Transparency · Data Minimization · Purpose Limitation · Access & Accuracy · Security

Other Malaysian Cyber Laws:

  • Cyber Security Act 2024
  • Electronic Commerce Act 2006 — legal recognition of electronic messages
  • Computer Crimes Act 1997 (CCA 1997) — computer misuse is an offense
  • Digital Signature Act 1997 — regulates use of digital signatures
  • Communications and Multimedia Act 1998 (CMA 1998)
  • Copyright Act (Amendment) 1997

Computer & Network Security

Malware · Hackers · DoS · Ransomware · Phishing · AI Scams

Introduction

Computer and network security = protection of computer systems, networks, and data from unauthorized access, damage, or theft — both physical and digital. Without proper security: hacking, malware, financial loss, data breaches, reputational damage.

Malware Types
TypeDescription
VirusAttaches to other files/programs; spreads when opened
WormSpreads on its own through security holes in networked computers (no user action needed)
Trojan HorseSeemingly harmless program (game/utility) that secretly installs malicious code
SpywareSecretly collects information like keystrokes and web activity
AdwareDisplays unwanted ads; sometimes bundled with spyware
RansomwareLocks your files and demands payment to unlock them
RootkitHides itself and gives attacker deep/admin-level access to the system

Famous Case: Internet Worm (Nov 2, 1988) — Robert Tappan Morris Jr. (Cornell grad student) released a worm from MIT. Spread to significant numbers of Unix computers; caused crashes. Morris: suspended from Cornell, 3 years' probation, 400 hrs community service, $150,000 in fines.

Hackers — STAR TOPIC ★
Hacking

The act of gaining unauthorized access to a computer system, network, or data — often by exploiting system vulnerabilities.

Motivations: Profit, protest, challenge, or personal reasons

⬜ White Hat Hacker

Intent: Non-malicious — to improve security

Method: Works WITH permission; employed by companies to find vulnerabilities

Legal? YES — follows laws and ethical guidelines

Examples: Security consultants, ethical hacking teams (penetration testers)

⬛ Grey Hat Hacker

Intent: No malicious intention, but acts illegally

Method: Access systems WITHOUT permission; may report security flaws or expose unethical behaviour

Legal? NO — still breaks the law

Controversial: good intention, wrong method. Example: hacking an org they believe does illegal activity

🖤 Black Hat Hacker

Intent: Steal, damage, or make money

Method: Uses malware, scams, ransomware; often part of criminal networks; disrupt services, steal/sell data

Legal? NO — illegal and unethical

Examples: Identity thieves, ransomware attackers

Other Major Threats
⚡ Denial-of-Service (DoS) Attacks ▼

Definition: Cyberattack that makes a system, network, or service unavailable by flooding it with traffic/requests. Goal is NOT to steal information — it is to disrupt a server's ability to respond.

DDoS (Distributed DoS): Launched from many computers simultaneously (e.g. via a botnet)

Famous Case: 2016 Dyn DDoS Attack — Disrupted Twitter, Netflix, Amazon. Used Mirai botnet targeting Dyn (major DNS provider).

Why unethical: Denies service to legitimate users; can cause major financial and reputational damage

💰 Ransomware-as-a-Service (RaaS) ▼

Criminals sell ransomware tools to others — no advanced skills needed. Ready-made kits on the dark web. Attacks on companies, hospitals, universities → data loss, financial demands, service disruption.

Case: Colonial Pipeline (USA, 2021) — DarkSide group attacked Colonial Pipeline using RaaS. Major fuel shortages in USA. Colonial paid $4.4M in ransom (some recovered). National infrastructure held hostage.

📱 Phishing via WhatsApp/Telegram ▼

Attackers send fake messages (banks, delivery services, job offers). Victims tricked into clicking malicious links or giving personal data. Case (Malaysia 2022-2023): Fake job offers (liking videos for pay), phishing sites, fake Shopee branding.

🤖 AI-Generated Scams (Deepfakes) ▼

Scammers use AI to fake voices, images, or videos — deepfakes. More believable, harder to detect.

Case 1 (UK, 2019): Deepfake CEO voice scam → company tricked into transferring USD243,000

Case 2 (Hong Kong, 2024): Deepfake CFO voice → $25 million transferred to fraudulent account

📉 Social Media Data Breaches ▼

Hackers steal personal data (names, phone numbers, private messages). Data sold, leaked, or used for identity theft. Case: Facebook Data Leak (2021) — 533 million users' data (2018-2019) posted on hacking forum. Meta fined US$275 million. Exploited a system vulnerability.

Professional Ethics

Professionalism · ACM Code · SE Code · MBOT · Whistleblowing · Act 711

Profession & Professionalism

Profession

A career requiring formal education and practical training. Has a public role and serves society. Regulated by professional bodies. Examples: Medicine, Law, Engineering, IT.

Narrow definition: Law, Medicine, Dentistry, Engineering, Architecture, Accountancy (require professional degrees + certification to practice)

Professional

A person qualified and trained in a profession. Expected to act ethically, uphold trust, and follow codes of ethics. Their decisions impact others — moral/ethical considerations are central.

IT Position: Relatively young field; many roles require degrees but not all require licensing — ongoing debate about formal professional status.

ACM Code of Ethics — STAR TOPIC ★

ACM = Association for Computing Machinery. Global, USA-based. Four sections:

Section 1: General Ethical Principles ▼

A computing professional should:

  • 1.1 Contribute to society and human well-being
  • 1.2 Avoid harm
  • 1.3 Be honest and trustworthy
  • 1.4 Be fair and take action not to discriminate
  • 1.5 Respect the work required to produce new ideas, inventions, creative works, and computing artifacts
  • 1.6 Respect privacy
  • 1.7 Honor confidentiality
Section 2: Professional Responsibilities ▼
  • 2.1 Strive for high quality in both processes and products
  • 2.2 Maintain high standards of professional competence, conduct, and ethical practice
  • 2.3 Know and respect existing rules pertaining to professional work
  • 2.4 Accept and provide appropriate professional review
  • 2.5 Give comprehensive evaluations of computer systems, including analysis of possible risks
  • 2.6 Perform work only in areas of competence
  • 2.7 Foster public awareness and understanding of computing and its consequences
  • 2.8 Access computing/communication resources only when authorized or compelled by public good
  • 2.9 Design and implement systems that are robustly and usably secure
Section 3: Organizational Leadership Principles ▼
  • 3.1 Ensure the public good is the central concern during all professional computing work
  • 3.2 Articulate and evaluate fulfillment of social responsibilities
  • 3.3 Manage personnel and resources to enhance quality of working life
  • 3.4 Articulate, apply, and support policies reflecting the Code's principles
  • 3.5 Create opportunities for organizational members to grow as professionals
  • 3.6 Use care when modifying or retiring systems
  • 3.7 Recognize and take special care of systems integrated into society's infrastructure
Section 4: Compliance with the Code ▼
  • 4.1 Uphold, promote, and respect the principles of the Code
  • 4.2 Treat violations of the Code as inconsistent with membership in the ACM

Whistleblowing — STAR TOPIC ★
Whistleblowing

When someone reports illegal, unethical, or harmful activities happening within an organization. A last resort — NOT a first step; NOT about complaining.

✓ Good Motives: Genuine desire to protect public interest; acting out of ethical responsibility

⚠ Questionable Motives: Retaliation or revenge; avoiding personal punishment

DeGeorge's Criteria for Ethical Whistleblowing: All 5 conditions must be met for whistleblowing to be a MORAL DUTY

#Criterion
1Is serious public harm involved?
2Have you reported the issue to your supervisor/manager?
3Have you used all internal reporting channels?
4Do you have strong evidence (documents, records)?
5Is there a reasonable chance whistleblowing will stop the harm?

Some ethicists: Only conditions 1–3 needed. Others: Whistleblowing is never morally required — personal choice.

Whistleblower Protection Act 2010 (Malaysia — Act 711):

  • Approved: 6 May 2010 | Came into effect: 15 December 2010
  • Encourages reporting corruption without fear
  • Implemented by: Police, Customs & Immigration, MACC, Ministries, gov departments
  • Protections: Confidentiality of identity; Immunity from civil/criminal action; Protection from detrimental action (e.g. dismissal, harassment)
  • Must report in good faith — full immunity does NOT apply to knowingly false reports
  • Can report internally or externally (to law enforcement/regulators). Public disclosure to media is NOT protected unless approved.

Situational Factors: (1) Will it lead to real corrective action? (2) Offender's position/influence? (3) Safer alternatives available? (4) Your financial stake in the company? (5) Legal risks of staying silent?

MBOT (Malaysia Board of Technologists)

Established under Technologists and Technicians Act 2015. Registers, recognizes, and regulates technologists and technicians in Malaysia. Members from IT, Engineering Technology, Applied Sciences.

MBOT Code of Conduct

A registered professional must not, knowingly or recklessly, assist another person in violating any accepted standards, laws, rules, or regulations related to professional services. Includes: helping break rules, ignoring misconduct, or failing to report unethical behavior.

Ethical Impact of IT on Work & Society

Automation · Telework · Globalization · Digital Divide · Employee Health

Automation

Using technology to do tasks with little/no human help. IT automation uses software to make systems/tasks run on their own. AI can now perform translation, diagnosis, writing, decision-making.

Ethical Issues:

  • Is it ethical to automate if it leads to massive job losses?
  • Who should be let go and who should stay?
  • What happens to those who lose their jobs?

Employer responsibilities: Transparency about changes; compensation; reskilling and retraining workers

Case: Amazon Warehouse — Robots sort/move/pack; workers monitored by algorithms tracking productivity. Those falling behind automatically flagged or terminated.

Malaysia: 620,000 employees will be affected by AI trends (2024 report)

Telework

Working outside the office using digital tools. Also: remote work, telecommuting, WFH. Common during COVID-19. Hybrid work is a new concept.

ProsCons
Saves time & cost (commute)Isolation & loneliness
Flexible schedule / better work-life balanceRisk of overwork / burnout
Reduces absenteeismBlurred work-personal boundaries
Supports sustainabilitySecurity risks from home setups
Helps retain talentDifficult to monitor productivity fairly

Ethical Issues: Employer vs Employee tension around privacy, monitoring, fairness

Employer: Respect privacy; support home setups; avoid overwork; communicate clearly

Employee: Use company resources responsibly; maintain professionalism; separate personal from work

Digital Divide

The gap between people who can access/use technology and those who cannot. Divided by: country, region, income, age, education.

Global Divide: Richer countries have better internet and devices access. IT infrastructure, literacy, and language matter.

Social Divide: Younger, more educated people tend to have better access and skills within the same country.

IT professional responsibility: Design inclusive technologies; promote fairness and accessibility to all

Globalization

Increased global connection through trade, technology, and culture. Technology helps businesses work across countries and time zones.

AdvantagesDisadvantages
More jobs in developing countriesLocal businesses lose to big foreign companies

★ Critical Focus

SE Code of Ethics

  • 8 principles: PUBLIC, CLIENT & EMPLOYER, PRODUCT, JUDGMENT, MANAGEMENT, PROFESSION, COLLEAGUES, SELF
  • Mnemonic: "PCPJudgment-MPCself" or "PC P MPC S"
  • PUBLIC: act with public interest — first principle, most important
  • PRODUCT: highest professional standards
  • JUDGMENT: maintain independence and integrity
  • SELF: lifelong learning
  • All 8 must be applied consistently; conflicts resolved in favor of public interest
★ Critical Focus

Whistleblowing

  • Definition: reporting illegal/unethical/harmful activity within org
  • NOT complaining; NOT first step; LAST RESORT
  • DeGeorge's 5 criteria: harm, supervisor, internal channels, evidence, reasonable chance
  • Act 711 (Malaysia): protection for good-faith reporting
  • Protections: identity confidentiality, civil/criminal immunity, no retaliation
  • Where to report: internally first, then to enforcement agencies
  • Public disclosure to media NOT protected without approval
  • From ethics to whistleblowing: code → see wrong → try internally → external needed
★ High Priority

Kantianism

  • Founded by Immanuel Kant
  • Moral laws derived through REASON — not feelings or consequences
  • Good will: doing right because it's right (not for gain)
  • Categorical Imperative (CI) — unconditional rule
  • 1st Formulation: Can the rule be universalized? If universalizing creates contradiction → WRONG
  • 2nd Formulation: Do I treat people as ends or as means? Using people as tools → WRONG
  • WORKABLE theory — uses rational reasoning, can be explained
  • Focus: ACTION, not outcome
★ High Priority

All 6 Ethical Theories

  • Subjective Relativism: personal opinion; NOT workable
  • Cultural Relativism: cultural norms; NOT workable
  • Ethical Egoism: self-interest; NOT workable
  • Divine Command: God's commands; NOT workable for persuasion
  • Kantianism: duty + universal rules; WORKABLE ✓
  • Act Utilitarianism: consequences per action; WORKABLE ✓
  • Rule Utilitarianism: consequences of rules for all; WORKABLE ✓
  • Social Contract: agreed rules for mutual benefit; WORKABLE ✓
  • Exam tip: always state whether theory is workable or not
Good to Know

Privacy & Information Classification

  • Privacy = control over personal life/info
  • Privacy dilemma: need to share to use services, but lose control
  • Personal info: IC, phone, health, bank — voluntary/involuntary/statutory
  • Public info: once personal, now accessible — still has privacy concerns
  • Public record: official bodies (birth cert, criminal records)
  • Solove's Taxonomy: Collection → Processing → Dissemination → Invasion
  • Key ethical principles: Autonomy, Consent, Transparency, Data Minimization, Purpose Limitation

Sem 2 2024/2025 — Section B & C
Q1: How does Malaysia handle spam-related messages, and under what conditions can the sender be punished?
4 marks

Malaysia does not have a specific anti-spam law, but several existing laws can be applied:

  • Communications and Multimedia Act 1998 (CMA), Section 233(1)(b): Spam is not explicitly illegal, but if used to annoy, threaten, or harass people, it can be punished with up to 1 year in jail or RM50,000 fine.
  • Personal Data Protection Act (PDPA) 2010: Illegal to collect, use, or sell personal data (e.g. phone numbers) without the person's permission. Punishment: up to RM500,000 fine or 3 years in jail.
  • Penal Code Section 507A (Stalking law): Sending repeated unwanted messages that cause fear or stress can be classified as stalking — up to 3 years in jail or a fine.

Conditions for punishment: The message must be intended to annoy, abuse, threaten, or harass, and must contain obscene, false, menacing, or offensive content. Commercial spam is generally a gray area because intent to harass is hard to prove.

Q4(b): The PDPA 2010 gives several rights to individuals. Explain any TWO of these rights and how they help protect personal data.
6 marks

Right 1: Right to Access Personal Data

This allows an individual to request and view the personal data that a company holds about them. It ensures transparency and allows the person to verify that the data is being used properly. For example, a bank customer can request to see what personal information the bank has stored.

Right 2: Right to Withdraw Consent

Individuals can retract their permission for a company to process their personal data. This gives people control even after they have initially provided their data. For example, if a user no longer trusts a mobile app with their data, they can withdraw consent and request that the company stop using it.

(Other acceptable answers: Right to Correct Personal Data; Right to Prevent Processing Causing Damage; Right to Prevent Processing for Direct Marketing)

Q5(b): Explain ONE situation where whistleblowing may be considered ethical, and ONE where it may be considered unethical. Provide an example for EACH.
6 marks

Ethical Situation: Whistleblowing is ethical when it is motivated by genuine concern for the public interest and all internal channels have been exhausted without result. According to DeGeorge's criteria, if serious public harm is involved, evidence exists, and there is a reasonable chance the report will prevent harm — whistleblowing becomes a moral duty.

Example: A software engineer discovers that the company's medical device has a critical security flaw that could endanger patients' lives. After raising the issue internally and being ignored, the engineer reports it to the relevant regulatory authority. This is ethical because it protects public safety.

Unethical Situation: Whistleblowing is unethical when motivated by personal revenge or used as a way to harm a colleague without valid grounds. Reporting without sufficient evidence or in bad faith undermines trust and damages innocent reputations.

Example: An employee is passed over for promotion and, out of resentment, reports false allegations of misconduct against a colleague to the management. This is unethical because the motive is revenge, not protection of the public interest, and the report lacks evidence.

Section C — Q1: Sofia the Software Engineer. Evaluate using Kantian ethics — First Formulation.
5 marks

Action under study: Sofia's manager asked her to transfer user health data to a third-party marketing company without users' specific consent.

Moral rule: "It is acceptable to share users' personal data with third parties without their consent for marketing purposes."

Universalization: What if every company did this? All organizations would routinely share user data without consent. Users would never be able to trust digital services with their personal information. The healthcare and technology industries would collapse in credibility, and people would stop using digital health apps altogether.

Verdict: The rule destroys itself when universalized — trust is impossible in a world where everyone shares data without consent. Therefore, according to Kant's First Formulation, this action is morally wrong. Sofia should NOT proceed with the task.

Section C — Q1(ii): Sofia — Second Formulation of Kantian Ethics
5 marks

Second Formulation: Treat people as ends in themselves, never merely as means to an end.

In this scenario, HealthSecure is using its 200,000+ users as a means to generate profit through marketing, without their knowledge or specific consent. The users shared their personal health data for the purpose of tracking their health — not for marketing. By diverting this data, the company reduces users to mere instruments for commercial gain.

This violates the Second Formulation because it fails to respect the users' dignity and autonomy. They have been deceived about how their data would be used, and they have no opportunity to refuse or withdraw consent for this specific purpose.

Verdict: Sofia should NOT proceed, as the action treats users as tools for profit, violating their dignity and autonomy — which is morally wrong under Kant's Second Formulation.

Section C — Q1(b): Using TWO principles from the ACM Code of Ethics, evaluate whether Sofia should proceed.
5 marks

Principle 1.2 — Avoid Harm: The ACM Code states that computing professionals must avoid causing harm to others. Sharing users' sensitive health data with a marketing company without their consent can cause real harm — including loss of privacy, emotional distress, and potential discrimination (e.g. health data used to deny insurance). Sofia proceeding with this action would contribute to this harm. Therefore, the ACM Code supports that she should NOT comply.

Principle 1.6 — Respect Privacy: The ACM Code explicitly requires professionals to respect the privacy of individuals. Users consented to share their health data only for tracking purposes — not for marketing. Transferring this data to a third party violates the privacy they reasonably expect. Sofia's role as a professional requires her to uphold this principle and refuse the instruction.

Conclusion: Both ACM principles support the position that Sofia should refuse the task. She may also need to consider internal reporting or whistleblowing if the manager insists.

Sem 1 2025/2026 — Selected Questions
Q1: Explain the difference between ethics and etiquette. Give ONE example for each in IT context.
6 marks

Ethics: A set of moral principles that guide what is right and wrong behavior, analyzed through rational examination. In IT/professional contexts, it refers to codes of conduct governing responsible and fair behavior. Ethics involves deeper moral considerations about harm, fairness, and rights.

IT Example: A software developer refuses to insert a hidden data tracking feature in an app without user consent, because it violates users' right to privacy — an ethical decision.

Etiquette: Social conventions for polite, respectful behavior in a given context — not necessarily related to deep moral principles, but about courteous conduct.

IT Example: A software engineer acknowledges a colleague's code contribution in a code comment or documentation — this is professional etiquette, showing courtesy and respect, but is not a deeply moral issue.

Key Difference: Ethics deals with fundamental right and wrong (moral judgments); etiquette deals with socially acceptable polite behavior. An action can be ethical but violate etiquette, or vice versa.

Q7: Describe TWO motives that may drive a person to hack and explain how these motives differ ethically.
6 marks

Motive 1: Financial Gain (Black Hat)

Some hackers are motivated by profit — they steal personal data, install ransomware, or sell access to compromised systems. This motive is ethically unjustifiable because it directly causes harm to victims (financial loss, data breach, emotional distress) and violates the rights of others for personal benefit. It is also illegal under laws such as the Computer Crimes Act 1997.

Motive 2: Exposing Vulnerabilities to Improve Security (White Hat)

Some hackers are motivated by a desire to improve system security. They break into systems with permission from the organization to identify weaknesses before malicious hackers can exploit them. This motive is ethically justifiable because it aims to protect others, is done with consent, and produces public benefit. These hackers follow legal and ethical guidelines.

Ethical Difference: The key distinction lies in intent, consent, and impact. Financial-gain hacking causes harm without consent (unethical); security-improvement hacking helps others with consent (ethical). Even when a hacker has good intentions but no permission (grey hat), the lack of authorization makes the action legally and ethically problematic.

Section C: AI Recruitment System — Apply Kantian Ethics to the company's moral responsibility.
5 marks

Action under study: The company uses an AI recruitment system trained on biased historical data, resulting in systematic discrimination against female and minority applicants.

1st Formulation (Universalizability): Moral rule: "It is acceptable for companies to use AI systems trained on biased data for hiring decisions." If universalized — every company would discriminate based on gender and ethnicity. Society would become structurally unfair and no qualified candidates from minority groups would have equal opportunities. This contradicts the very principle of fair recruitment. The rule destroys the concept of meritocracy → morally wrong.

2nd Formulation (Humanity as an End): The company is treating female and minority applicants as means to an end (efficiency and cost-cutting) rather than as individuals deserving fair evaluation. By allowing a biased algorithm to make decisions, the company denies these applicants their dignity and right to equal treatment. This fails to respect them as ends in themselves → morally wrong.

Conclusion: The company's use of the biased AI system is morally unjustifiable under Kantian ethics. They have a moral responsibility to audit, correct, or suspend the system until bias is eliminated.

Sem 3 2024 — C1(b): Based on PDPA Act 2010, analyze TWO user rights violated in the HealthSecure case.
5 marks

Violation 1: Right to Withdraw Consent to Process Personal Data

HealthSecure shared user data with third-party advertisers and researchers without obtaining specific consent. Under PDPA, users have the right to withdraw consent and determine how their data is used. Since users were not informed that their data would be shared for advertising, their consent was never properly obtained for this secondary purpose. This is a clear violation of the right to control how personal data is processed.

Violation 2: Right to Prevent Processing Likely to Cause Damage and Distress

HealthSecure stored users' unencrypted personal health and contact data on cloud servers without access control. This inadequate security led to a data breach affecting over 200,000 users. Under PDPA, users have the right to prevent companies from processing their data in ways that could cause harm. The storing of sensitive health data without encryption directly led to emotional distress, privacy violations, and potential risks to users' safety and dignity — all of which the PDPA is designed to prevent.

B5(b): Should you whistleblow on a co-worker's plan to release a banking app without full security? Apply TWO of DeGeorge's rules.
4 marks

Position: YES, whistleblowing should be considered.

DeGeorge's Criterion 1 — Serious Public Harm: A banking app released without full security poses a significant risk of financial fraud, unauthorized access, and data breaches affecting thousands of users. This meets the threshold of "serious public harm" — which is the primary condition for whistleblowing to be morally justified. The harm is not hypothetical; a banking app handles sensitive financial data and its failure could cause real financial and emotional damage to users.

DeGeorge's Criterion 2 — Reporting to Supervisor: Before whistleblowing externally, you must first raise the issue internally with your supervisor or team lead. If the supervisor acknowledges the risk but still instructs the team to proceed, this internal channel has been attempted. If internal channels fail to produce corrective action, external reporting becomes more morally justified.

Conclusion: Since the first criterion (serious public harm) is clearly met and internal reporting should be attempted first, whistleblowing — if internal channels fail — becomes a moral duty under DeGeorge's framework. As a software professional, the ACM Code also requires you to avoid harm (1.2) and design secure systems (2.9).
