Topic 1: Ethics and Underlying Philosophies

1. Simple Explanation

This chapter builds the foundation. First you learn what society, morality, and ethics mean. Then you learn how philosophers and ethical theories help solve moral problems in a structured way.

2. Full Notes

  • Society: organized group living under rules intended to improve members’ lives over time.
  • Morality: society’s rules of conduct about right/wrong, good/bad, fair/unfair.
  • Values and norms: honesty, justice, respect, integrity, compassion, punctuality, politeness, queuing, greetings.
  • Ethical point of view: respect not only your own goals but also other people’s goals and core values.
  • Ethics: rational examination of morality and evaluation of behaviour.
  • Technology creates new ethical situations, but core ethics still applies.
  • Scenario from slide: Alexis uses another student’s login at a private college library to gain an unfair academic advantage; useful for fairness, privacy, law, and access analysis.
  • Historical philosophers: Socrates, Plato, Aristotle.
  • Crito story: Socrates argues for obeying laws rather than escaping unjust punishment, emphasizing law, community, and tacit agreement.
  • Main ethical theories in syllabus: relativism, ethical egoism, divine command, Kantianism, utilitarianism, social contract theory.

3. Key Terms

TermMeaningSimple Example
SocietyOrganized group following rulesPeople living together under laws
MoralityShared rules of conductBelief that cheating is wrong
EthicsStudy of right and wrongReasoning whether data misuse is acceptable
Ethical frameworkStructured way to judge actionsUsing Kantianism in a case study
Tacit agreementAgreement without saying it directlySocrates staying in Athens

4. Why This Topic Matters

Almost every other topic depends on this chapter because the later chapters keep asking you to apply these theories and distinctions.

5. Exam Tips

  • Know the difference between morality, ethics, etiquette, and law.
  • Use Socrates/Crito if a question asks about obeying laws or social duty.
  • For theory questions, always define first, then apply.

6. Easy Memory Trick

Morality = social rules. Ethics = thinking about those rules.

7. Possible Exam Questions

  • Define society, morality, and ethics.
  • Explain Socrates’ argument about obeying laws.
  • Why is studying ethics important in IT?
Topic 2: Network Communication Issues

1. Simple Explanation

This chapter is about online communication problems: spam, censorship, identity theft, predators, misinformation, cyberbullying, and internet addiction.

2. Full Notes

  • Spam: mass distribution of unsolicited messages; may include advertising, scams, phishing, or malware.
  • Key spam characteristics: anonymous, mass-mailed, unsolicited.
  • Spammers use opt-in lists, dictionary attacks, breaches, rotating IPs, and image-based text to bypass filters.
  • CAN-SPAM Act 2003 categories: transactional emails, commercial emails, unsolicited commercial emails; main requirements include accurate sender, honest subject line, opt-out, address, ad notice.
  • 2024 update in slides: penalties up to USD 51,744 per email violation.
  • Malaysia: no specific anti-spam law; can use CMA, PDPA, Penal Code/stalking-related provisions when intent includes harassment, threats, fraud, or misuse of personal data.
  • Censorship: direct censorship and self-censorship; used for security, harmful content control, or political control.
  • Why internet censorship is hard: many-to-many communication, dynamic content, huge number of sites, cross-border jurisdiction, age verification difficulty.
  • Kant and Mill oppose censorship because it limits autonomy and truth-seeking.
  • Children and inappropriate content: web filters, parental controls, education, and laws like CIPA.
  • Trust issues: identity theft, online predators, false information, cyberbullying.
  • Internet addiction: compulsive online behavior despite harm; signs include failed control, neglecting duty, irritability offline, escapism.

3. Key Terms

TermMeaningSimple Example
SpamUnwanted bulk messageMass fake prize emails
PhishingFake message to steal dataFake bank login page
Direct censorshipExternal suppression of contentGovernment blocks a website
Self-censorshipVoluntary silence due to pressureJournalist avoids a topic
CyberbullyingRepeated digital harassmentRumors spread in class group chat

4. Why This Topic Matters

This topic matters because communication technology is powerful but easy to abuse. IT professionals build the systems where spam, moderation, privacy, and online harm happen.

5. Exam Tips

  • Spam questions often ask about law plus conditions for punishment.
  • For censorship questions, give both reasons for and reasons against.
  • For cyberbullying, stress repeated, intentional digital harm.

6. Easy Memory Trick

Topic 2 = communication risks: spam, speech, trust, addiction.

7. Possible Exam Questions

  • Explain why internet censorship is challenging.
  • How does Malaysia handle spam-related messages?
  • Define identity theft and cyberbullying.
Topic 3 Part 1: IP and Software

1. Simple Explanation

This chapter explains how law and ethics protect creators, inventions, brands, and software, while also balancing public benefit and access.

2. Full Notes

  • IP has commercial value and comes from the mind.
  • Four main protections: copyright, patent, trademark, trade secret.
  • Copyright usually belongs to the creator, but work created during employment usually belongs to employer.
  • Patent requires novelty, non-obviousness, and usefulness.
  • Trademark can become generic if the public uses it as a general term.
  • Trade secrets need active protection such as NDAs, confidentiality rules, limited access, passwords, encryption.
  • Ethical consequences of IP misuse: legal penalties, reputation harm, lost business, cease-and-desist, criminal charges in serious cases.
  • Fair use factors: purpose/character, nature, amount, effect on market.
  • Public domain routes: expiry, failure to renew, voluntary release/dedication, no copyright protection.
  • Creative Commons licenses make legal reuse easier; CC BY requires attribution; CC0 approximates public domain freedom.
  • Software piracy is unauthorized duplication/use; harms fairness and developer revenue.
  • Open-source software encourages collaboration; proprietary software restricts source code and modification.

3. Key Terms

TermMeaningSimple Example
Fair useLimited use without permission in some contextsUsing short quote in assignment
CC BYReuse allowed with attributionUse photo with creator credit
CC0Creator waives rights as much as possibleUse freely without asking
Soft liftingOne license shared on many computersInstalling one purchased app on office PCs

4. Why This Topic Matters

Students and IT professionals constantly use content and code from others. This topic teaches when that use is fair, licensed, or illegal.

5. Exam Tips

  • If a question mentions ‘claimed as his own’, discuss both IP violation and plagiarism/dishonesty.
  • Use the four fair use factors one by one.

6. Easy Memory Trick

IP is about ownership plus permission.

7. Possible Exam Questions

  • Explain the difference between copyright and patent.
  • What is software piracy and why is it unethical?
  • How do Creative Commons licenses work?
Topic 3 Part 2: Privacy, PDPA, Malaysian Cyber Laws

1. Simple Explanation

This chapter explains how personal information should be collected, stored, processed, and protected, especially in Malaysia.

2. Full Notes

  • Privacy is control over personal life and information; violating privacy insults dignity.
  • Information privacy focuses on how personal data is collected, stored, shared, and used.
  • Information classes: personal information, public information, public records.
  • Disclosures can be voluntary, involuntary, or statutory.
  • Solove taxonomy categories: collection, processing, dissemination, invasion.
  • Key case studies: eKYC and face recognition, phone number misuse, MySejahtera concerns.
  • Cyber laws in slides: Cyber Security Act 2024, PDPA 2010, Electronic Commerce Act 2006, Computer Crimes Act 1997, Digital Signature Act 1997, CMA 1998, Copyright Act, Telemedicine Act.
  • PDPA applies to personal data in commercial transactions and grants multiple rights to individuals.
  • Ethical privacy principles: autonomy/control, confidentiality, consent, transparency, data minimization, purpose limitation, access/accuracy, security.

Topic 6: Ethical Impact of IT on Work and Society

1. Simple Explanation

This chapter studies how IT changes jobs, work style, fairness, access, and health.

2. Full Notes

  • Automation improves efficiency but can displace workers and raise bias/accountability questions.
  • Employers should be transparent, compensate fairly, and support reskilling/retraining.
  • Telework gives flexibility and cost/time savings but can bring isolation, burnout, blurred boundaries, security issues, and monitoring concerns.
  • Ethical tension in telework: accountability vs privacy, productivity vs trust.
  • Employer responsibilities: respect privacy, support proper work setup, avoid overwork, communicate clearly.
  • Employee responsibilities: use resources responsibly, maintain professionalism, protect company data, separate personal and work responsibilities.
  • Ethical monitoring should be transparent, consensual, limited to work matters, fair, and lawful.
  • Globalization brings cheaper products and opportunity but can worsen labor exploitation, low wages, poor safety, and inequality.
  • Digital divide is unequal access to technology across countries and social groups; IT professionals should design inclusive systems.
  • Health effects: mental and physical risks such as stress, isolation, eye strain, bad posture, sleep problems.

PDPA 2010 Lecturer Focus

1. Simple Explanation

PDPA is Malaysia’s main law for protecting personal data used in commercial transactions. In easy words: companies cannot simply collect, use, share, or sell your personal data however they like.

2. Full Notes

  • Purpose: regulate processing of personal data in commercial transactions.
  • Slide emphasis: privacy means control over personal information; violating privacy insults dignity.
  • Personal data includes identifying information such as name, IC/passport, phone number, address, bank details, health details.
  • Sensitive personal data is especially risky data such as biometric or health-related information. The slides use IC scans and face recognition in eKYC as a key example of higher-risk data.
  • Data user: the entity that decides how and why personal data is processed.
  • Data subject: the individual whose data is being processed.
  • Data processor: a party that processes data on behalf of the data user.
  • Rights highlighted in the slides: access data, correct data, withdraw consent, prevent processing likely to cause damage/distress, prevent direct marketing.
  • Ethical privacy principles from slides: autonomy/control, confidentiality, informed consent, transparency, data minimization, purpose limitation, access and accuracy, security.
  • Malaysia examples from slides: bank eKYC using IC scans and face recognition, misuse of phone numbers after online contests/free Wi-Fi/e-commerce registration, MySejahtera third-party access concerns.
  • Poor privacy practice can cause identity theft, spam, emotional distress, dignity harm, distrust, data leakage, and legal consequences.

3. Key Terms

TermMeaningSimple Example
PDPA 2010Malaysia law regulating personal data in commercial transactionsA business must not reuse customer phone numbers for marketing without proper permission
Personal dataData that identifies a person directly or indirectlyFull name plus IC number
Sensitive personal dataHigh-risk personal data needing stronger careFace scan or health history in bank eKYC
Data userPerson or organization deciding how/why data is usedA bank collecting customer KYC data
Data subjectThe individual the data is aboutThe customer opening the bank account
Data processorParty processing data for the data userCloud vendor storing the bank’s data
ConsentPermission given by the individualTicking a clear marketing opt-in box

4. Why This Topic Matters

PDPA matters because IT systems constantly collect user data. In exam questions, privacy is usually not just legal; it is also about dignity, trust, and ethical control over information.

5. Exam Tips

  • If the question asks for PDPA rights, name the right and explain how it protects the person.
  • If the case involves marketing without permission, focus on consent, purpose limitation, and direct marketing.
  • If the case involves a breach, mention security, confidentiality, and loss of trust.
  • If you are unsure, use the safe answer frame: consent + purpose + minimal collection + access/correction + security.

6. Easy Memory Trick

Remember `ACWDS + CTDAPS`: Access, Correct, Withdraw, Distress, Stop marketing plus Consent, Transparency, Data minimization, Accuracy, Purpose limitation, Security.

7. Possible Exam Questions

  • Define PDPA and explain its purpose.
  • Differentiate personal data and sensitive personal data.
  • State any two PDPA rights and explain how they protect data subjects.
  • Discuss whether selling customer data to marketers without consent violates PDPA.
Hackers Lecturer Focus

1. Simple Explanation

A hacker is someone who gains access to systems by exploiting weaknesses. Not every hacker is the same. The exam cares a lot about intention, permission, and harm.

2. Full Notes

  • White hat: acts with permission to improve security; ethical and legal when authorized.
  • Grey hat: may expose vulnerabilities without malicious intent but still without permission; good intention, wrong method.
  • Black hat: steals, damages, extorts, or profits illegally; clearly unethical and illegal.
  • Script kiddies: less skilled users who rely on ready-made tools.
  • Hacktivists: hack to support a political or social cause.
  • Cybercriminals: hack for money, fraud, ransomware, or organized crime.
  • Insider threats: people inside the organization abusing authorized access.
  • Ethical hacking requires authorization, defined scope, and security purpose.
  • Difference between hacking and ethical hacking: permission is the biggest dividing line; ethical hacking also aims to improve security instead of harming systems.
  • Examples from slides include white hats, grey hats exposing unethical behavior, black hats using ransomware or scams.

3. Key Terms

TermMeaningSimple Example
HackerPerson carrying out hacking activitiesSomeone exploiting a server flaw
White hatAuthorized security testerPenetration tester hired by a bank
Grey hatUnauthorized but not purely malicious hackerPerson entering a site without permission then reporting the bug
Black hatMalicious illegal hackerRansomware gang stealing data
Ethical hackingSecurity testing with permissionCompany-approved vulnerability assessment
Insider threatInternal person abusing trust or accessEmployee copying customer database

4. Why This Topic Matters

Hackers matter in IT ethics because the same technical skill can be used to protect systems or to exploit people. Ethics here is about permission, intent, consequences, and respect for rights.

5. Exam Tips

  • Always mention permission when comparing white hat and grey hat.
  • If asked whether no-harm hacking is okay, say unauthorized access still violates trust and law.
  • For motive questions, contrast profit, protest, curiosity/challenge, and public-interest disclosure.

6. Easy Memory Trick

White = with permission. Grey = good intention, bad method. Black = bad intention and bad method.

7. Possible Exam Questions

  • Compare white hat, grey hat, and black hat hackers.
  • Explain why grey hat hacking is ethically controversial.
  • Differentiate hacking and ethical hacking.
Intellectual Property Lecturer Focus

1. Simple Explanation

Intellectual property protects creations of the mind. In this subject, always ask: what is being protected, who owns it, and whether someone used it without permission.

2. Full Notes

  • IP covers original ideas/creations with commercial value.
  • Copyright protects creative expression such as books, songs, films, images, and software code/executable/screen displays.
  • Patent protects inventions, systems, and useful new ideas; usually 20 years from filing; must be applied for and approved.
  • Trademark protects signs identifying brands such as names, logos, slogans, sounds, or colors; can continue with renewal and use.
  • Trade secret protects valuable business information kept secret, like formulas, processes, customer lists, and internal data.
  • Fair use/fair dealing in the slides: limited use for education, research, criticism, news, etc., judged by purpose, nature, amount, and market effect.
  • Public domain: works no longer protected or never protected; free to use, but a compiled collection may still be protected.
  • Creative Commons: creators share with conditions; CC BY requires attribution; CC0 gives maximum freedom.
  • Software piracy means unauthorized duplication or use of software; types include soft lifting, uploading/downloading, counterfeiting, renting, hard disk loading, OEM unbundling.
  • Open-source vs proprietary: open-source gives access/modification rights under license; proprietary restricts source code and use.
  • Plagiarism is presenting someone else’s work as your own; even if the material is easy to access online, using it without proper credit is unethical.

3. Key Terms

TermMeaningSimple Example
CopyrightProtects original creative expressionSource code of an app
PatentProtects inventions and functional innovationsA new hardware cooling design
TrademarkProtects brand identifiersA company logo or slogan
Trade secretProtects secret business informationCoca-Cola formula style example

4. Why This Topic Matters

IP matters in IT because students, developers, designers, and companies constantly use code, images, videos, datasets, and software. Wrong use can be both unethical and illegal.

5. Exam Tips

  • If asked to compare copyright and patent, stress expression vs invention and automatic vs application.
  • If a case mentions code from GitHub or an online image, discuss license terms and attribution.
  • For software piracy, explain why it harms creators, fairness, and innovation.

6. Easy Memory Trick

C-P-T-T = Copyright expression, Patent invention, Trademark brand, Trade secret hidden business advantage.

7. Possible Exam Questions

  • Differentiate copyright, patent, trademark, and trade secret.
  • Explain the four fair use factors.
  • Why is software piracy unethical?
  • Discuss Alex using a CC BY image without attribution.
ACM Code of Ethics Lecturer Focus

1. Simple Explanation

The ACM Code tells computing professionals how they should behave. In exams, it is very useful for case studies because it gives named principles you can directly apply.

2. Full Notes

  • General ethical principles in slides: contribute to society and human well-being; avoid harm; be honest and trustworthy; be fair and take action not to discriminate; respect the work required to produce new ideas/inventions/creative works/computing artifacts; respect privacy; honor confidentiality.
  • Professional responsibilities: strive for high quality; maintain competence and ethical practice; know and respect rules; accept/provide review; evaluate systems and risks thoroughly; perform work only in areas of competence; foster public awareness; access resources only when authorized or for public good; design secure systems.
  • Leadership principles: public good first; support social responsibility; manage people/resources to improve working life; support code-based policies; create growth opportunities; use care when modifying/retiring systems; take special care of infrastructure systems.
  • Compliance: uphold, promote, and respect the code; treat violations seriously.

3. Key Terms

TermMeaningSimple Example
Avoid harmDo not create or allow preventable damageDo not push unsafe software
Respect privacyProtect personal information and user controlDo not sell health data without consent
Honor confidentialityKeep entrusted information privateDo not leak client records
Fair / non-discriminationAvoid unfair bias or exclusionDo not let AI unfairly reject women/minorities
High qualityAim for reliable professional workTest an app properly before release

4. Why This Topic Matters

ACM is important because it converts vague ethics into direct professional duties. It is one of the best tools for answering case studies on privacy, security, fairness, quality, and professional conduct.

5. Exam Tips

  • Pick the most relevant two principles and apply them directly to the facts.
  • Do not only list the principles. Explain how the action violates or supports them.
  • Common useful ACM principles for case studies: avoid harm, honesty, fairness/non-discrimination, respect privacy, confidentiality, high quality, system risk evaluation, secure design.

6. Easy Memory Trick

Remember the main first-block principles as `Well-being, harm, honesty, fairness, work, privacy, confidentiality`.

7. Possible Exam Questions

  • State any two ACM principles and apply them to a privacy case.
  • How can ACM be used in an AI bias case study?
Software Engineering Code of Ethics Lecturer Focus

1. Simple Explanation

This code is specifically for software engineers. The exam usually wants you to know all eight principles and explain them in simple language.

2. Full Notes

  • PUBLIC: act consistently with public interest.
  • CLIENT AND EMPLOYER: act in best interests of client/employer consistent with public interest.
  • PRODUCT: ensure products and related modifications meet highest professional standards possible.
  • JUDGMENT: maintain integrity and independence in professional judgment.
  • MANAGEMENT: managers/leaders should promote ethical development and maintenance practices.
  • PROFESSION: advance integrity and reputation of software engineering consistent with public interest.
  • COLLEAGUES: be fair to and supportive of colleagues.
  • SELF: lifelong learning and promoting ethical practice.

Whistleblower Lecturer Focus

1. Simple Explanation

2. Full Notes

  • Whistleblowing is reporting illegal, unethical, or harmful activity.
  • It is not ordinary complaining; it is about serious misconduct affecting people, safety, or public trust.
  • Internal whistleblowing: report inside the organization.
  • External whistleblowing: report to regulators, law enforcement, or authorized enforcement bodies.
  • Anonymous whistleblowing: report without revealing identity; may reduce retaliation risk but can affect credibility/evidence handling.

  • Benefits: protects public, exposes fraud, strengthens accountability.
  • Risks: retaliation, damaged career, stress, team breakdown, legal issues, organizational conflict.

Ethical Theories Lecturer Focus

1. Simple Explanation

Ethical theories are decision frameworks. They help you explain why an action is right or wrong instead of giving only personal opinion.

2. Full Notes

  • Subjective relativism: each individual decides right/wrong; not workable because it gives no shared standard.
  • Cultural relativism: society/culture decides right/wrong; not workable because it makes criticism of harmful cultural practices difficult.
  • Ethical egoism: act in self-interest; not workable because it encourages selfishness and conflict.
  • Divine Command Theory: right/wrong depends on God’s commands; limited in multi-faith or secular persuasion contexts.
  • Kantianism: follow universal moral rules and treat people as ends; workable.
  • Act utilitarianism: choose action with greatest net happiness; workable but can permit troubling outcomes.
  • Rule utilitarianism: follow rules that generally produce the most happiness; workable and more stable.
  • Social contract theory: morality comes from agreed rules for mutual benefit, rights, and duties; workable.
