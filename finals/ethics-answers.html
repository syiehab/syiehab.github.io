Section BSubjective Questions — 50 Marks

Socrates, through Plato's Crito, argued that citizens who benefit from the state's laws enter an implicit social contract and are morally obligated to obey those laws — or work to change them through legitimate persuasion rather than defiance. His views remain highly relevant to digital ethics today:

  1. Social contract obligation in cyberspace: Internet users benefit from digital infrastructure, platform services, and legal frameworks that protect them online. This creates a Socratic duty to comply with cybersecurity laws (Computer Crimes Act 1997), data protection regulations (PDPA 2010), and intellectual property law — not merely because enforcement is possible, but because it is ethically owed.
  2. Legitimate channels over digital vigilantism: Socrates believed in using persuasion and speech to change bad laws. In digital contexts, this means advocating against censorship or surveillance laws through democratic channels — rather than hacking government infrastructure or conducting cyberattacks as protest.
  3. Moral duty persists despite anonymity: The internet provides anonymity unavailable in ancient Athens, yet Socrates' view implies that anonymity does not reduce moral obligation. A person who pirates software, conducts phishing attacks, or spreads malware cannot justify the act because they will not be caught.
  4. Tension with unjust digital laws: Socrates acknowledged that deeply unjust laws may warrant principled defiance at great personal cost — relevant to digital rights advocates facing authoritarian censorship — though he counselled extreme restraint.
  5. Professional accountability for IT practitioners: Socratic ethics reinforces that professional codes of conduct (ACM Code of Ethics) represent a social contract with the public. Compliance is a moral duty, not merely a legal one.
Conclusion: Socrates' social contract reasoning applies broadly to digital citizenship — the obligation to obey digital laws, use legitimate dissent, and uphold professional ethics all find grounding in his philosophy.
Subjective Relativism

Moral judgments are relative to the individual. Each person is their own sole moral authority. What is right or wrong depends entirely on one's personal beliefs and attitudes. There are no objective moral truths — only individual perspectives, all equally valid.

Cultural Relativism

Moral judgments are relative to the culture or society a person belongs to. An action is right or wrong only within the context of the cultural norms of a particular group. No culture can claim moral superiority over another; ethics is defined by shared social norms, not universal principles.

The CAN-SPAM Act of 2003 establishes legally binding rules for commercial email in the United States. The scenario — emails with the subject line "You've Won a Prize!" for promotional purposes — violates the Act as follows:

  1. Prohibition on deceptive subject lines (Section 5): The Act explicitly prohibits subject lines that are misleading about the contents or purpose of the email. "You've Won a Prize!" implies the recipient has won something when it is a promotional advertisement — a direct violation of the requirement that subject lines honestly reflect the message's content.
  2. Prohibition on false or misleading header information: All routing information (From, To, Reply-To) must accurately identify the sender. If the company disguises its identity, this is a further violation.
  3. Mandatory identification as an advertisement: The email must clearly disclose that it is a commercial advertisement. Framing it as a prize notification without this disclosure violates this requirement.
  4. Mandatory opt-out mechanism: Every commercial email must include a clear, conspicuous mechanism for recipients to opt out of future messages, honoured within 10 business days.
  5. Physical postal address: A valid physical postal address of the sender must be included in the email.
Penalties: Each violation can attract fines up to $50,120 per email. The deceptive subject line is a clear violation of Section 5 and exposes the company to significant civil and potential criminal liability.

Copyright is an exclusive, automatic legal right protecting original creative works from unauthorised reproduction, distribution, or modification. In the digital world, it applies across different forms of IP:

  1. Software and source code: Computer programs are treated as literary works and protected automatically upon creation. This prevents competitors from copying, decompiling, or distributing proprietary code. Open-source licences (GPL, MIT) use copyright to enforce licence terms.
  2. Digital images and photography: Photographs and digital artwork are automatically protected from the moment of creation. Using an artist's image on a website or commercial product without permission constitutes infringement regardless of whether a copyright notice is displayed.
  3. Music and audio content: Both the musical composition (melody, lyrics) and the specific sound recording are separately copyrightable. Unauthorised downloading, streaming, sampling, or sharing of music violates these rights.
  4. E-books, articles, and digital text: Written works published digitally retain full copyright. Copying and republishing substantial portions of an e-book or academic article without permission infringes the author's exclusive rights.
  5. Audiovisual and video content: Films, online videos, and multimedia works are protected. Uploading movies or TV episodes without authorisation is infringement, enforceable through DMCA takedowns and platform content identification systems like YouTube ContentID.

Daniel Solove's Taxonomy (2006) identifies 16 privacy-related activities in four categories: Information Collection, Information Processing, Information Dissemination, and Invasion.

Where the Taxonomy is useful for social media:

  1. Aggregation problem: Social media platforms combine individually harmless data points — name, employer, location check-ins, likes, friends lists — into detailed sensitive profiles used for targeted advertising and political microtargeting. Solove's taxonomy explicitly identifies aggregation as a distinct privacy harm, giving regulators and critics precise vocabulary to articulate this concern.
  2. Secondary use: Data collected for one purpose (social connection) is repurposed for commercial profiling or sale to data brokers. The taxonomy treats secondary use as a harm independent from original collection, helping regulators argue that consent for one use does not imply consent for all uses.
  3. Surveillance and increased accessibility: Continuous tracking of user behaviour and making previously limited-audience content globally searchable are both captured within the taxonomy — directly applicable to social media architecture.
  4. Disclosure and distortion: Sharing private posts without consent and algorithmic amplification of false or damaging information fall within Solove's information dissemination harms.

Limitations:

  • Developed before modern AI-driven platforms existed; does not address deepfakes, biometric surveillance, or algorithmic behaviour manipulation.
  • Descriptive, not prescriptive — identifies harms but does not specify legal or technical remedies.
Conclusion: Solove's Taxonomy remains highly useful for categorising social media privacy harms, but requires supplementation to address AI-era and platform-specific challenges.

Impact of a malware attack:

  • Data breach and theft: Malware (spyware, keyloggers, RATs) can exfiltrate sensitive customer data, trade secrets, and financial records — exposing the company to PDPA 2010 penalties and civil liability.
  • Operational disruption: Ransomware encrypts critical systems and halts business operations entirely. Recovery can take days or weeks, causing severe financial loss.
  • Data integrity compromise: Some malware modifies or corrupts data without deletion, undermining the reliability of business records.
  • Reputational and financial damage: A publicised breach erodes customer confidence, triggers regulatory scrutiny, and may result in class-action litigation.

Prevention strategies:

  1. Regular software patching and vulnerability management: Apply security patches promptly to close known vulnerabilities exploited by malware.
  2. Employee security awareness training: Train staff to recognise phishing emails and social engineering — the primary malware delivery vectors.
  3. Endpoint protection and network defences: Deploy EDR tools, firewalls, and intrusion detection systems (IDS) to detect and block malicious activity at endpoint and network level.
  4. Encrypted off-site backups (3-2-1 rule): Maintain three copies of data on two media types with one off-site or offline, enabling recovery without paying ransom.
  5. Network segmentation and least privilege access: Divide the network into isolated zones and restrict user permissions to the minimum necessary for each role.
  1. Privacy by Design (PbD): Integrating privacy protections from the earliest stages of development — conducting Privacy Impact Assessments (PIAs), minimising data collected to what is strictly necessary, and building consent management and access controls into the architecture from the start. How it helps: Ensures legal compliance with PDPA 2010 from launch, reduces costly post-release fixes, and builds user trust by treating privacy as a core value rather than an afterthought.
  2. Structured code review with an ethical risk checklist: Mandatory peer review of all code before deployment — evaluating not only for bugs but specifically screening for security vulnerabilities, discriminatory algorithmic outcomes, and misuse risks. Using a formal ethics checklist ensures that issues like insecure data storage or overly broad data collection are caught before reaching users. How it helps: Prevents harm to users, avoids regulatory liability, and creates institutional accountability for ethical decision-making within the team.

Section CHealthSecure Case Study — 20 Marks

Kant's Categorical Imperative has two key formulations: (1) Universal Law — act only on maxims you could will to be universal laws; and (2) Humanity Formula — always treat persons as ends in themselves, never merely as means.

Point 1 — Sharing data without consent cannot be universalised (Universal Law):

HealthSecure's maxim: "A health app may share users' sensitive personal data with third-party advertisers without specific consent." If universalised — if every health application did this — the concept of informed consent would become meaningless. Users would stop trusting health apps with their data altogether, collapsing the ecosystem HealthSecure itself depends upon. The maxim is self-defeating; it cannot be universalised without contradiction. Therefore, sharing data without consent is morally unjustifiable under Kant's First Formulation.

Point 2 — Storing unencrypted data treats users merely as means (Humanity Formula):

By failing to encrypt data and implement access controls, HealthSecure treated users' sensitive health information as a low-cost resource to exploit rather than as data belonging to autonomous persons deserving protection. Users were not informed of the security inadequacy and could not consent to the risk. Kant's Humanity Formula is violated — users were treated as data commodities to facilitate HealthSecure's business model, not as rational beings with dignity and rights.

Point 3 — Removing attribution from copyrighted code involves deliberate deception, which Kant absolutely prohibits:

The developer took another person's code, removed all attribution, and claimed the feature as original. Kant holds that deception is categorically impermissible under all circumstances. Additionally, applying the Universal Law test: if every developer universally appropriated others' code and claimed it as their own, the entire foundation of open-source collaboration and intellectual attribution would collapse. No rational developer would share work under such conditions. The maxim is self-defeating, and the act involves a direct lie — morally unjustifiable under both formulations.

Conclusion: None of HealthSecure's actions — unauthorised data sharing, insecure storage, or deceptive code attribution — is morally justifiable under Kantian ethics. All three fail the universalisability test and violate the requirement to respect persons as rational ends in themselves.
  1. Right to be Informed and Right to Consent (Sections 6 & 7, PDPA 2010): Under the PDPA, data users must clearly inform data subjects of the purposes for which their data is collected and must obtain explicit consent before processing it for any purpose — particularly any secondary use beyond the original stated purpose. HealthSecure collected sensitive health data under the guise of a free health tracking app but secretly shared it with third-party advertisers and researchers. Users were never informed of this secondary use and never provided specific consent for it. This directly violates their right to be informed and their right to control how their personal data is used.
  2. Right to Security and Protection from Unauthorised Access (Section 9, PDPA 2010): The PDPA requires data users to implement reasonable, practical security measures to protect personal data from loss, misuse, modification, unauthorised access, or disclosure. HealthSecure stored highly sensitive health and contact data of over 200,000 users in plaintext (unencrypted) on cloud servers with no access controls — a fundamental and egregious failure of this duty. The resulting data breach is direct evidence of this violation. Every user whose data was exposed had their right to have personal data protected securely violated.

Legal analysis: Open-source code on GitHub is governed by specific licence terms (MIT, Apache 2.0, GPL, etc.). All standard open-source licences — and Creative Commons Attribution (CC BY) licences — require that attribution to the original author be retained in all copies or substantially derived works. By removing this attribution, the developer breached the licence terms. A licence breach voids the permission to use the code and converts the use into copyright infringement — making the reuse legally unauthorised and actionable under copyright law.

Ethical analysis: Even setting aside legality, deliberately removing attribution and falsely claiming another person's work as original constitutes deliberate deception. It denies the original developer due recognition, violates the trust underpinning open-source collaboration, and constitutes professional misrepresentation — an ethical violation independent of and additional to the legal one.

Conclusion: The developer's action was neither ethical nor legal. It breached the open-source licence (copyright infringement), involved deliberate deception (removing attribution), and falsely claimed another person's intellectual work as original — failing both legal standards and basic professional ethics.

Technical measure — End-to-end encryption and strict access control: Implement AES-256 encryption for all personal data stored at rest on cloud servers, and TLS encryption for all data in transit. Deploy Role-Based Access Control (RBAC) enforcing the principle of least privilege — ensuring only authorised personnel with a legitimate need can access specific categories of sensitive health data. Conduct regular penetration testing and vulnerability assessments to identify and remediate weaknesses proactively.
  2. Organisational measure — Data governance framework and PDPA compliance programme: Establish a comprehensive data governance policy covering data classification, a formal consent management system, and a documented incident response plan. Appoint a Data Protection Officer (DPO) to oversee PDPA 2010 compliance, deliver regular staff training on data handling obligations, and conduct Privacy Impact Assessments (PIAs) before deploying any new features involving personal health data.

Software Piracy

The unauthorised copying, distribution, installation, sale, or use of software without a valid licence from the developer or publisher. This includes downloading cracked software, using a single licence key on more machines than permitted, sharing licence keys, and creating or distributing counterfeit copies of commercial software.

Why it is considered unethical:

  • Theft of intellectual property: Software represents enormous investments of skilled labour, creativity, and capital. Copying it without authorisation is analogous to stealing a physical product — the creator is denied compensation they legitimately earned.
  • Violation of the developer's exclusive rights: Copyright law grants developers the exclusive right to determine how their work is distributed, priced, and used. Piracy overrides this autonomy without consent, violating both the legal right and the underlying moral right of creators to control their own work.
  • Harm to the development ecosystem: Lost revenues reduce resources for research and development, security patches, and support — legitimate users ultimately bear this cost through higher prices and reduced software quality.
  • Unfair competitive advantage: Businesses using pirated software gain an illegitimate cost advantage over competitors who pay for licences, distorting fair market competition.
  1. Financial harm to the software industry and innovation: Software piracy directly deprives developers and publishers of licence sale revenue. For large companies, this reduces funds for R&D, resulting in slower innovation. For small independent developers, widespread piracy can be existential — directly driving them out of business. These losses are ultimately passed on to consumers through higher prices for legitimate software.
  2. Serious security and safety risks to users of pirated software: Pirated software is routinely distributed through unofficial channels where malicious actors inject malware, ransomware, spyware, or backdoors into installation packages. The user who pirated the software may become the primary victim — having files encrypted for ransom, banking credentials stolen, or their device conscripted into a botnet. Pirated software also cannot receive official security patches, leaving known vulnerabilities permanently unpatched.

Whistleblowing

Whistleblowing is the act by which a current or former member of an organisation — typically an employee — discloses information about illegal, immoral, unsafe, or otherwise illegitimate practices occurring within that organisation to a person or body with the authority or ability to take corrective action. Disclosure may be internal (to senior management or a compliance officer) or external (to regulators, law enforcement, or the public). Whistleblowing typically involves personal risk to the disclosing individual, including potential retaliation or job loss, and is generally motivated by concern for the public interest or protection of affected parties.

Situation Where Whistleblowing May Be Ethical

When it may be ethical: When serious harm to the public is imminent or ongoing, internal reporting has been genuinely attempted and failed, credible evidence exists, and the motivation is protecting the public interest rather than personal gain.

Example: A cybersecurity analyst at a hospital discovers that the patient management system stores all patient medical records — including HIV statuses and psychiatric histories — in plaintext with no encryption or access controls. She reports this to IT management on three occasions over two months with no action taken. She then files a formal report with Malaysia's Department of Personal Data Protection (JPDP) and Ministry of Health. This is morally justified: the harm to patients is serious and imminent, internal channels were genuinely exhausted, evidence is concrete, and her motivation is to protect patients who cannot protect themselves.

Situation Where Whistleblowing May Be Unethical

When it may be unethical: When the act is primarily motivated by personal revenge or financial self-interest rather than genuine public concern; when it disproportionately harms innocent third parties; or when the alleged wrongdoing is minor and internal channels were never genuinely attempted.

Example: A software developer passed over for promotion anonymously leaks the company's proprietary source code and confidential client contracts to a competitor, framing it as "exposing poor security practices." In reality, the code contains no significant vulnerabilities, the dismissal was lawful, and the motivation is personal grievance. The disclosure harms innocent colleagues and client organisations. This is malicious sabotage disguised as whistleblowing and is morally unjustifiable.

First Formulation: "Act only according to that maxim whereby you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law."

The maxim of Sofia's action (if she proceeds): "A software engineer may transfer users' personal health data to a third-party marketing company without specific consent, when instructed to do so by management for commercial purposes."

Test of universalisability: If this maxim were adopted universally — if every software engineer in every health app company transferred user data to marketing companies whenever commercially instructed — users would recognise that health apps cannot be trusted with their personal data and stop sharing sensitive health information entirely. Second Formulation: "Act so that you treat humanity, whether in your own person or in that of another, always as an end and never as a means only."

Users of the health application are rational agents who made a deliberate, informed decision to share sensitive personal data — location, medical symptoms, and health history — for one specific purpose: to receive a personalised health service. Users have a fundamental right as rational beings to determine how their most sensitive personal data is used. Overriding this without consent reduces them to means for the company's commercial purposes. Sofia must refuse.

The ACM Code requires computing professionals to take care to avoid causing unnecessary harm to others. Transferring users' sensitive health data to a marketing company without consent causes real identifiable harm: invasion of privacy, psychological harm from unwanted commercial profiling, potential discriminatory targeting based on disclosed health conditions, and financial harm if data is misused. Sofia participating in this transfer would make her a direct contributor to these harms. The Code obligates her to refuse any instruction that causes unjustified harm to third parties, regardless of whether it comes from her immediate supervisor.

The ACM Code explicitly requires computing professionals to protect the privacy of others and to use personal information only in ways consistent with the terms under which it was collected. The app's users shared their health data under a specific agreement — for the purpose of receiving a personalised health service. Using that data for marketing falls entirely outside the scope of the original consent, directly violating the conditions users reasonably and legitimately relied upon. Subjective Relativism

Moral judgments are entirely relative to the individual. Each person is their own sole moral authority; there are no objective moral truths. What is right for one person may not be right for another, and no external standard can validly override a person's sincere personal moral judgment.

Ethical Egoism

The moral theory that each person ought to act in whatever way best promotes their own self-interest. The morally correct action is always the one that produces maximum personal benefit for the actor. The interests and wellbeing of other people have no independent moral weight in the actor's decision-making.

How each theory might justify software piracy:

  • Subjective relativism: If an individual sincerely believes that copying software without paying for a licence is acceptable, then that belief makes piracy morally right — for them. No objective external standard can override this personal moral view. The software developer's contrary belief that their rights are being violated is simply that developer's personal opinion, which carries no more validity than the pirate's own view.
  • Ethical egoism: If pirating software costs nothing, the risk of legal prosecution is low, and the software fully meets the individual's needs, then the pirated copy produces maximum personal benefit at minimal cost or risk. Ethical egoism would endorse this action on the grounds that it maximises self-interest. The financial loss inflicted on the software developer is morally irrelevant to the actor under this framework.
Critique: Both theories fail as comprehensive ethical frameworks because they cannot consistently condemn clearly harmful acts. A world where everyone followed ethical egoism and pirated all software would eliminate financial incentives to develop software — ultimately destroying even the pirate's long-term access to the software they wish to use.
  1. Financial harm and security damage through fraud, phishing, and malware delivery: Spam is the primary delivery vector for phishing attacks, advance-fee fraud, malware, and ransomware. Individuals deceived into clicking malicious links or opening infected attachments may suffer identity theft, emptied bank accounts, ransomware-encrypted devices, and significant financial losses. The global economic cost of spam-facilitated cybercrime runs into the hundreds of billions of dollars annually.
  2. Productivity loss and infrastructure resource consumption: Spam wastes enormous productive time and technical resources across society. Individuals and organisations collectively spend billions of hours annually filtering and deleting spam. Email servers require substantial computational resources and bandwidth to process spam before delivery — costs borne by internet service providers, organisations, and ultimately consumers. Anti-spam infrastructure itself represents a massive ongoing financial investment redirected from productive purposes.

Jurisdictional and cross-border enforcement difficulty: The vast majority of spam originates from foreign countries — frequently jurisdictions with minimal cybercrime legislation, limited law enforcement capacity, or hostile relationships with the spam-receiving country. A spam campaign targeting Malaysian users may originate from servers routing through multiple intermediate countries to obscure origin. By the time any investigation reaches a meaningful stage, spammers have typically relocated infrastructure to a different jurisdiction or rebuilt under a new identity, rendering enforcement practically ineffective.

Whistleblowing

Whistleblowing is the act by which a current or former member of an organisation — typically an employee or contractor — discloses information about illegal, unsafe, unethical, fraudulent, or otherwise illegitimate practices occurring within that organisation to a person or entity with the authority or capacity to take corrective action. Disclosure may be internal (to senior management or an ethics hotline) or external (to government regulators, law enforcement, or the public). It typically involves significant personal risk including potential professional retaliation, termination, or legal action. It is generally considered to be motivated by concern for the public interest or protection of harmed parties, rather than personal gain or vendetta.

Morally Required — Reason & Example

Reason: When a computing professional has credible evidence of a serious, imminent danger to the public that cannot be resolved through internal channels, and whistleblowing is the only remaining means of preventing that harm, it becomes a moral duty. De George's conditions support this: if the harm is serious, internal reporting is genuinely exhausted, evidence is credible, and external disclosure has a reasonable chance of preventing harm, whistleblowing is morally required.

Example: A senior IT security engineer at a large Malaysian bank discovers a critical vulnerability in the online banking platform that allows attackers to bypass multi-factor authentication and access any customer account. She reports this to the Head of IT Security, the CISO, and the COO over six weeks. Each time she is told the fix is being "prioritised" but no action is taken and the platform remains live. She then formally reports to Bank Negara Malaysia's cybersecurity unit. This is morally required: the harm to banking customers is serious and imminent, internal channels were genuinely and persistently exhausted, evidence is concrete, and she has strong reason to believe regulatory intervention will force resolution.

Morally Questionable — Reason & Example

Reason: Whistleblowing may be morally questionable when primarily motivated by personal grievance or financial self-interest rather than genuine public concern; when the disclosure causes disproportionate harm to innocent third parties; or when internal channels were never genuinely attempted and the alleged wrongdoing is trivial.

Example: A contracted software developer is dismissed following a legitimate poor performance review. Motivated by personal anger, he publicly posts the company's proprietary source code to an open GitHub repository and sends the link to a technology journalist, framing it as "exposing reckless security practices." In reality, the code contains no significant vulnerabilities and the dismissal was entirely lawful. The disclosure harms innocent colleagues and clients, constitutes a criminal breach of his NDA, and serves no genuine public interest — the motivation is personal revenge. This is malicious sabotage dressed in the language of ethical disclosure.

Section CCase Study — AI Recruitment System (20 Marks)
Utilitarianism (Bentham / Mill): An action is morally right if and only if it produces the greatest good (utility, wellbeing) for the greatest number of people affected by it. All parties' interests must be considered impartially.

Arguments in favour (claimed benefits):

  • The system increases hiring efficiency, processing thousands of applications faster than human reviewers — reducing costs and administrative burden for the company.
  • Management claims it reduces subjective human biases in the review process.

Arguments against (actual harms identified):

  • Discriminatory outcomes affecting a significant group: Internal audits reveal the AI disproportionately rejects female and minority applicants even when equally qualified. These individuals suffer concrete, serious harm: loss of legitimate employment opportunities, economic disadvantage, psychological harm from discrimination, and reinforcement of systemic inequality. These are not trivial harms — career outcomes affect financial security and social mobility over decades.
  • Harm is systemic and self-perpetuating: The model trained on historically biased data encodes and perpetuates existing social and gender discrimination at scale. Rather than reducing bias, the system amplifies it across every hiring cycle.
  • Claimed benefits do not fully materialise: A system that systematically excludes qualified candidates does not maximise utility — it deprives the company of talent and perpetuates workplace monoculture, undermining long-term organisational health.
Utilitarian conclusion: The AI recruitment system is not morally justified under utilitarianism. A comprehensive calculation including all affected parties — particularly systematically excluded female and minority applicants — reveals that the harm caused (discrimination, economic exclusion, perpetuation of inequality) substantially outweighs the efficiency gains enjoyed by the company. The greatest good for the greatest number is not achieved when a system encodes discrimination against identifiable groups.

First Formulation — Universal Law:

The company's maxim: "A company may deploy an AI recruitment system trained on historically biased data, without adequate pre-deployment bias testing, because it increases processing efficiency." Can this be universalised? If all companies universally deployed biased AI hiring systems without adequate bias auditing, meritocratic recruitment would be replaced by algorithmic discrimination institutionalised across entire labour markets. Female and minority workers would be systematically excluded regardless of qualifications, and the principle of equal opportunity would become meaningless. No rational society could will this as a universal law — it is self-evidently contradictory to the principles of fair employment all companies publicly claim to uphold. The action fails the universalisability test.

Second Formulation — Humanity Formula:

Every job applicant is a rational human being with goals, dignity, and the right to be evaluated on their actual qualifications and capabilities. By deploying an AI system that rejects female and minority candidates due to the composition of past hiring cohorts rather than individual merit, the company treats these applicants not as ends in themselves but as interchangeable data points to be processed by a machine trained on unjust historical patterns. Their individuality, qualifications, and dignity as rational persons are entirely subordinated to statistical biases embedded in the model. This directly violates the Humanity Formula: the company has a moral responsibility to ensure every applicant is evaluated fairly as an individual, not as a member of a historically underrepresented demographic category.

Kantian conclusion: The company bears clear moral responsibility for deploying a system that fails the Universal Law test and treats applicants as data commodities rather than as rational persons deserving individual, merit-based evaluation. The company had a duty to audit for bias before deployment and a continuing duty to correct it immediately upon discovery.
ACM Principle 1.4 — Be Fair and Take Action Not to Discriminate

The ACM Code requires computing professionals to be fair and to actively take action to prevent discrimination based on characteristics such as gender, race, or ethnicity. The AI recruitment system demonstrably and disproportionately discriminates against female and minority applicants — individuals possessing equivalent qualifications to those shortlisted. The company not only failed to prevent this discrimination through adequate pre-deployment testing; it allowed the system to operate for several months after deployment before internal audits revealed the problem, and presumably continued to use discriminatory results throughout. This is a serious violation: the company's IT and AI development teams had a professional obligation to audit the system for discriminatory outcomes before deployment — and to act immediately upon discovery.

ACM Principle 2.5 — Give Comprehensive and Thorough Evaluations of Computer Systems and Their Impacts, Including Analysis of Possible Risks

The ACM Code requires computing professionals to conduct thorough evaluations of computer systems before deployment, with particular attention to potential risks to users and affected parties. The company failed to conduct adequate evaluation of the AI model's potential for discriminatory outcomes before deploying it at scale. A responsible evaluation would have included bias auditing of training data, fairness testing across demographic groups, independent review of the model's decision criteria, and assessment of risks to applicants from automated rejection. Bypassing or inadequately performing this evaluation — and deploying a system encoding known historical biases without verification — is a clear violation of the professional obligation to rigorously assess risks before systems affecting real people's lives and careers are released.

The company should take the following actions, considering both professional responsibility and fairness to all applicants:

  1. Immediate suspension and independent algorithmic audit: Suspend the AI system from making or influencing final hiring decisions immediately, pending a thorough independent audit conducted by external AI ethics specialists — not the company's own development team. The audit must document all sources of bias in the training data, identify the specific features driving discriminatory outcomes, and quantify the scale of harm to affected demographic groups. All applications rejected during the period of biased operation should be identified, and a process established to fairly review those rejections — potentially re-opening consideration for candidates unfairly excluded.
  2. Rebuild the model with debiased, representative training data and embedded fairness constraints: Retrain the machine-learning model using a curated, balanced dataset that does not reflect historical hiring patterns rooted in past discrimination. Work with AI ethics specialists and diversity experts to implement algorithmic fairness constraints — such as demographic parity or equal opportunity metrics — ensuring the system evaluates candidates based solely on job-relevant qualifications. Testing for discriminatory outcomes across all demographic groups must be a mandatory, documented step in any future AI deployment lifecycle.
  3. Mandate human oversight for all consequential decisions and establish a transparent appeals process: No job application should be definitively rejected solely by automated means. A mandatory human review stage must be introduced for all AI-screened candidates. The company must establish a transparent appeals process allowing applicants to challenge automated decisions and have their applications reviewed by a trained, impartial human evaluator. This reflects the professional responsibility to ensure that consequential decisions affecting individuals' careers are made by accountable persons, and the basic fairness owed to every applicant as a rational person entitled to individual consideration.
  4. Establish accountability structures and communicate transparently: Appoint a dedicated AI Ethics Officer responsible for ongoing monitoring and bias auditing of all AI systems. Publish a transparency report acknowledging the bias issue, describing corrective actions taken, and committing to regular independent audits. Inform affected applicants that their applications were processed by an AI system subsequently found to exhibit bias and provide them with the opportunity to reapply under fair conditions.
Professional responsibility conclusion: Addressing algorithmic bias is not merely a reputational risk management exercise — it is a fundamental professional and moral obligation. Computing professionals who build systems affecting millions of people's lives bear responsibility for the fairness of those systems. Organisations deploying such systems must create accountability structures capable of identifying and correcting harms before they cause irreversible damage to affected individuals' careers and livelihoods.
