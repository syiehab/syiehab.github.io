CH 1

Introduction to Critical Infrastructure & ICS

VERY CRITICAL
📊 Past Year Pattern This chapter appears in ALL 3 past year papers. Q1 in Section B of every exam asks about ICS definition, tasks, and components. MCQ always tests CI definition, ICS types, IT vs OT, and CIA triad priority.

1.1 What is Critical Infrastructure (CI)?

Definition: Assets (real or virtual), systems and functions that are vital to the nation, whose incapacity or destruction would have a devastating impact on:

  1. Public health and safety
  2. National economic strength
  3. National defense and security
  4. National image
  5. The government's capability to function
📌 Exam Tip The definition question is worth 1–2 marks. Always mention "assets vital to the nation" and the 5 impact areas. Memorize these 5 impacts — they appear in MCQ options.

Malaysian CI Sectors (CNII – defined by CyberSecurity Malaysia):

  1. National defense and security
  2. Banking and finance
  3. Information and communication
  4. Energy
  5. Transportation
  6. Water
  7. Health services
  8. Government
  9. Emergency services
  10. Food and agriculture

US Homeland Security CI Sectors (16 sectors) — examples for MCQ:

Chemical, Commercial Facilities, Communication, Dams, Energy, Financial Services, Food & Agriculture, Healthcare, IT, Nuclear, Transportation, Water & Wastewater.

1.2 What is an Industrial Control System (ICS)?

Definition: A broad class of automation systems used for control and monitoring functionality in manufacturing and industrial facilities.

Why ICS matters for cybersecurity: ICS is extensively used in many critical infrastructures. The use of ICS is the main reason why critical infrastructure requires cyber security protection.

Types of ICS (commonly used in CI):

TypeFull NameKey Role
PCSProcess Control SystemControls industrial processes
DCSDistributed Control SystemDistributed across many nodes
SCADASupervisory Control and Data AcquisitionRemote monitoring & control
SISSafety Instrumented SystemSafety shutdown systems

General Tasks of ICS (memorize these 3 — they appear in exam every year):

  1. Collect data from endpoint devices
  2. Send collected data to an HMI to be displayed
  3. Apply automatic, semi-automatic or operator-controlled changes to endpoint devices

1.3 ICS Components

Components in an ICS are commonly referred to as assets. There are two categories:

Field Components:

  • Sensors
  • Actuators
  • Motor drives
  • Gauges
  • Indicators

Control System Components (these are the exam targets):

ComponentFull NameFunction
PLCProgrammable Logic ControllerSpecialized industrial computer that automates functions within manufacturing facilities
RTURemote Terminal UnitCollects data from field devices and transmits to central monitoring station. May also relay control signals back to field devices. Requires communication capability.
IEDIntelligent Electronic DeviceMicroprocessor-based controllers for power system equipment (circuit breakers, transformers). Issues control commands (e.g. tripping a circuit breaker). Used for substation automation.

📌 Exam Tip – Q1(c) repeated every year "Provide THREE (3) components of ICS and explain each." Always choose PLC, RTU, and HMI (or IED). Give a full explanation for each — 2 marks each = 6 marks total. Include the key function!

1.4 Industrial Protocols

Protocols used by ICS components to communicate with each other.

Proprietary Protocols:

  • Honeywell CDA
  • General Electric SRTP
  • Siemens S7

Non-proprietary / Licensed Protocols:

  • OPC, Modbus, DNP3, ICCP, CIP, PROFIBUS, IEC 60870-5-101/104
📌 Key Exam Point – "Security Through Obscurity" Proprietary protocols rely on "security through obscurity" — keeping the protocol secret so no one knows how to exploit it. This does NOT work anymore because: (1) attackers can always reverse-engineer proprietary protocols, and (2) most equipment now uses open/non-proprietary protocols for interoperability — everyone knows how they work. This is a very common MCQ!

Why ICS protocols are easy to exploit: They were originally designed for serial communication within isolated ICS networks. Security was NOT a concern since they were assumed to only be used within the ICS. When adapted for TCP/IP and the Internet, they became vulnerable.

Why IT security practices don't apply well to ICS:

  1. Old systems cannot simply be patched or upgraded
  2. Downtime for security updates may not be acceptable
  3. Not possible to have development and test environments
  4. Cannot apply strict access control (systems need easy operator access)
  5. Cannot always use conventional antivirus or firewall
  6. ICS protocols cannot be blocked due to safety/production needs
  7. Government-regulated industries — security adds complexity and cost

1.6 ISA-95 / IEC 62264 Levels in ICS

ISA-95 Purdue Model (Simplified): Level 4: Enterprise/Business Network (ERP, Business Systems) ↕ [IT/OT boundary - firewall] Level 3: Site Operations (MES, Historian, Supervisory) Level 2: Supervisory Control (SCADA, HMI servers) Level 1: Basic Control (PLCs, RTUs, IEDs) Level 0: Field Devices (Sensors, Actuators, Motors)

ICS operates at Levels 0–3; IT operates at Level 4. The convergence of these networks is the main security risk.

CH 2

ICS History, Trends & Cyber Threats

VERY CRITICAL

2.1 Brief History of ICS

  • Before 1960s: Manual monitoring (gauges on pipes)
  • Early ICS: Analog — pneumatic (air/steam pressure) and hydraulic (water/fluid) controls
  • Modern ICS: Made possible by transistors and modern electronics
  • Early digital era: Many vendors, proprietary technology, no standards
  • Over time: Vendors reduced via mergers; standard protocols adopted for interoperability
  • IT integration: Business systems required process control data → network convergence began
  • Final evolution: Convergence with IP networks

Why early ICS protocols were "lite" (limited/insecure):

  1. Need to minimize data due to slow communication channels
  2. No room for resiliency or security protection
  3. Security was not a concern — protocol assumed to only be used within ICS

Benefits of IP convergence:

  1. Use cheap and widely available IP network equipment
  2. Easier to deploy and support (available skill pools)
  3. Facilitates interfacing with IT business systems
  4. Better, faster, cheaper monitoring and management
⚠️ The Problem with Convergence Convergence of ICS onto IP platforms comes with increased security risk. Commercial off-the-shelf (COTS) hardware/software is used — the same vulnerabilities that affect business IT now affect ICS.

2.2 The Air Gap — What Is It?

Air gap: Physical separation between the industrial network and business/external networks, with no common electronic system crossing the gap.

Historical claim: ICS was "air-gapped" so cyber threats from the internet couldn't reach it.

Reality: Since the 1990s, organizations began integrating business applications with ICS → the air gap was bypassed. Separation is now done through VLANs and firewalls, not true physical separation.

📌 Key Exam Concept "Any path that exists can be found and exploited." The air gap no longer provides security. This idea links to the Stuxnet case study — Stuxnet bypassed the air gap via USB drives (physical media). In 2525/26 exam, Q6(b) asks about zones and conduits — this is the modern replacement for the air gap.

2.3 Cyber Threat Evolution

Historical Milestones:

  • 1902: First security attack — Marconi Wireless Telegraph system
  • 1988: Morris worm (first major internet worm)
  • 2001: Code Red | 2003: Slammer | 2008: Conficker | 2010: Stuxnet

Trends in ICS Cyber Threats:

  1. Most attacks are opportunistic, some are targeted
  2. Majority of ICS incidents are unintentional (software/control bugs)
  3. Initial attacks use simpler exploits; if thwarted, become more sophisticated
  4. Majority of attacks are financially motivated; espionage and sabotage are common too
  5. Most attacks originate externally using weak or stolen credentials
  6. New malware samples increasing rapidly and becoming more sophisticated
  7. AutoRun malware (from USB) bypasses network security perimeters
  8. "Hacking as a Service" increasingly available
  9. Incidents via remote access are increasing

Most Common Initial Attack Vectors:

  1. Spear phishing — targeted phishing at specific individuals
  2. Watering hole — infecting websites the target is likely to visit
  3. Database injection — SQL injection or similar

After infection — "Pivoting":

Malware propagates to other systems to: find new systems with higher authorization, find lower-level devices, and find specific devices to attack.

2.4 Advanced Persistent Threat (APT)

APT Definition: Malware that is difficult to detect and can continue to operate even if detected and removed or the system is rebooted (persistent).

APT also describes cyber campaigns where the attacker actively infiltrates systems or exfiltrates data using multiple, parallel infiltration vectors.

APT vs Weaponized Malware — Key Comparison:

📌 Exam Tip — MCQ Favourite "Modern malware is considered APT because it is very difficult to detect, remove and cease its operation." This is the MCQ answer! Not because of "advanced hackers with persistent motivation."

2.5 Stuxnet — The Landmark Case Study

Stuxnet (discovered 2010) is the most important case study in this subject. It appears in almost every exam question.

How Stuxnet Avoided Detection:

  1. Used zero-day exploits not detectable prior to its discovery (bypassed host IDS)
  2. Disguised itself as legitimate software
  3. Covered its tracks by removing trace files
  4. Automatically removed itself from a host if it was not the intended target (after infecting other hosts a specific number of times)

Why Stuxnet is "Weaponized" Malware:

  1. Highly sophisticated and extremely targeted
  2. Clear purpose: discover, infiltrate and sabotage a specific target system
  3. Utilized multiple zero-day exploits for infection
  4. Used "insider intelligence" to focus on its target control system (Siemens PLC)
📝 Assignment 1 Connection Assignment 1 required investigating a famous CI attack (Stuxnet, BlackEnergy, WannaCry, Colonial Pipeline, or recent attack). The exam will likely test your ability to describe these attacks in structured question format.

CH 3

Threats to the ICS

VERY CRITICAL
📊 Past Year Pattern Threats chapter tested in Q2 of every past year paper. Key questions: "Differentiate direct vs indirect threats", "List threat agents", "Policy to protect ICS from outsiders", "BYOD risks", "Social engineering".

3.1 Threat Definitions

Threat: A possible danger that might exploit a vulnerability to breach security and therefore cause possible harm.

Threat Agent: An individual or group that can cause a security threat (can be anyone from a malicious hacker to an inept computer operator to a rat chewing a data cable).

5 Actions a Threat Agent Can Take Against an Asset:

  1. Access — unauthorized access
  2. Misuse — unauthorized use of assets
  3. Disclose — illegally disclose sensitive information
  4. Modify — make unauthorized changes to an asset
  5. Deny access — prevent authorized access

3.2 Motivations of ICS Attacks

For general IT attacks — MONEY is the main motive:

  1. Steal banking data (identity theft, credit card fraud)
  2. Steal intellectual property to sell
  3. Sell services of compromised computers (spam, DDoS)
  4. Hold data hostage for ransom (ransomware)

For ICS attacks — SABOTAGE is an additional significant motive:

  1. Cause utility outages (blackouts)
  2. Cause manufacturing to stop (cease production)
  3. Damage equipment
  4. Cause equipment to malfunction
  5. Cause injuries and death

📝 Exam Answer Format — 4 Marks

Direct Threat (2 marks): A direct threat involves unauthorized access to ICS devices such as sensors, controllers, or HMIs. The attacker gains control over the device, enabling them to execute, interrupt, cancel, delay, or change control and monitoring operations. [Example: An attacker sends malicious commands to a PLC to shut down a water pump]

The impact is unintentional but can still be disastrous. [Example: Network scanning malware generates large amounts of traffic that overwhelms an ICS network, causing unintended denial of service]

3.6 Malware Spread & BYOD Risks

How devices get infected with malware:

  1. Downloading questionable software (pirated, games, music)
  2. Clicking bad links to malicious websites
  3. Opening infected email attachments
  4. Inserting infected USB drives (Bad USB)

BYOD (Bring Your Own Device) risks:

  1. Same device used at home AND at work → infection can come from home
  2. Device connects to both corporate network (WiFi) AND external ISP (mobile data)
  3. Mobile data bypasses network security perimeter
  4. Firewall and web filtering don't work when using mobile data
  5. When device switches to corporate WiFi — infection can spread to corporate network
📝 Assignment / Exam Scenario — BYOD "Linda is a programmer at KTM and uses BYOD..." — This scenario from past year Q6 tests BYOD risks. The risk is that Linda's personal device, used both at home and work, can introduce malware into the critical rail system. The BYOD policy bypasses network perimeters.

Why even the best antivirus fails:

  • Statistics show even the best antivirus allows 40% of malware to pass undetected
  • Antivirus relies on signatures of past malware — new malware has no signature yet
  • Existing malware can always be altered to pass signature tests

Why malware is so difficult to detect:

  1. Arrives in packed format, unrecognizable to detection systems
  2. Once installed, malware can disable antivirus and IDS
  3. Reverts to packed form; hides deep in file system (system, hidden, read-only attributes)
  4. Some malware can morph itself during replication (code variants to avoid detection)

3.7 Social Engineering

Steps in modern social engineering attack:

  1. Compromise a social network account (via compromised device, sloppy passwords, insecure WiFi)
  2. Leverage trusted social network to infect others
  3. Send phishing message to contact/friend list — "trusted" because it comes from a "friend"
  4. Lure receiver to visit malicious website or download file
  5. Once new account infected, process repeats (propagation)
📝 Exam Scenario — "Jonny at Singapore Power" "Jonny shares his work ID card on Facebook/Instagram and checks in his location." Assets at risk: his identity (can be used for physical access), his location (enables targeted physical attacks), potentially revealing CI facility details. Mitigation: Social media policy, security awareness training, ban sharing of work-related information on social media.
CH 4

Vulnerabilities of the ICS

VERY CRITICAL
📊 Past Year Pattern Chapter 4 is heavily tested in EVERY exam. Functional vulnerabilities (DoV/LoV/MoV/DoC/LoC/MoC), data states, and the 3 categories of vulnerabilities (Business/Operational/Technical) are repeated questions.

4.1 ICS Data States

ICS data exists in three states: at rest, in use, and in motion.

StateDefinitionICS PrioritySafeguard
Data at RestRetained within memory/storage of a device (non-volatile — retained even if turned off). Databases, hard disk, USB drive.Low — historical data value decreases rapidlyEncryption, access control, physical security
Data in UsePresent in volatile memory or displayed on screen. Disappears if device turned off. ICS devices have limited memory/processing — little scope for manipulation.MediumSystem integrity, access control
Data in MotionMoving through a network in packets (each packet may take different routes). May be encrypted (rare in ICS).HIGHEST — ICS depends on live operation dataEncryption, authentication, integrity checks

Data in Motion — How it can be compromised:

  1. Injecting erroneous packets
  2. MITM: intercept → transform to data at rest → change data → send back out
📌 Past Year Q3(c) SEM 1 24/25 "Name THREE (3) types of data states and describe the mechanism available to safeguard the data in each state." — 6 marks. 2 marks each. For each: name the state, define it, name the safeguard mechanism.

4.2 ICS Vulnerability Categories (3 Types)

Security control features are categorized into: Business (Management), Operational (or Technical). Vulnerabilities follow the same 3 categories:

📋 Business / Management Vulnerabilities (click to expand) ▼
  1. Enterprise Risk Management (ERM) not implemented — without ERM, resources for ICS security are hard to obtain; no link between ICS security and corporate goals
  2. Lack of policies defining roles and responsibilities — ownership of ICS security poorly defined; without responsibility, no accountability
  3. Ad hoc or one-time investment — ICS security is a program (continuous), not a point-in-time exercise; budget must be allocated every year
  4. Lack of management engagement — management should provide guidance such as: refer to NIST 800-82, use defense in depth, identify access control requirements, use cryptography
📋 Operational Vulnerabilities (click to expand) ▼
  1. Lack of segregation between ICS and business data traffic — malware can travel unimpeded into ICS
  2. Lack of separation of duties for administrative accounts — sharing admin accounts, complete overlap of duties, common passwords → impossible to attribute accountability or perform forensics
  3. Lack of remote access procedures — third-party suppliers need proper control over remote access accounts
  4. Poor wireless system design/architecture — susceptible to eavesdropping and masquerade attacks
  5. Lack of hardening for Internet-connected devices — poorly hardened systems can easily be compromised
  6. Lack of incident detection, response and reporting procedure — without proper evaluation procedure, consistent response is impossible; reports should contain quantifiable metrics
  7. Lack of change management controls — system upgrades, patches, reconfiguration must be managed carefully
  8. Lack of acceptance testing and vulnerability testing — new assets must be tested to ensure no new vulnerabilities are introduced
📋 Technical Vulnerabilities (click to expand) ▼

Sources for technical vulnerabilities: CVE database (cve.mitre.org) and antivirus/security companies.

ICS devices have two distinct communication interfaces:

  1. IP communication interface — where operators issue commands; subject to remote/local cyber attacks; vulnerabilities cause: denial/loss/manipulation of VIEW
  2. I/O control interface — physically connected to field devices (sensors, valves, drives); controls physical functions through analog communication; vulnerabilities cause: denial/loss/manipulation of CONTROL

4.4 Technical Attack Vectors (8 Types)

  1. IT devices on ICS network — Compromised IT devices serve as staging points; ICS suffers collateral damage
  2. Interdependency with IT — ICS workstations use Windows/Unix; same protocols → IT devices can "illegally" operate in ICS network
  3. Green network stacks — Immature and poorly tested network protocols/IP stacks in ICS devices; vendors rushed products to market assuming isolation
  4. Legacy ICS protocols — Still actively used due to long device lifecycle; designed without security consideration
  5. Limited processing power/memory — Cannot be upgraded with security enhancements; replacement requires shutdown (unacceptable); new devices may introduce unknown risks
  6. Storms / DoS attacks — Flood devices with excessive traffic; causes DoV/DoC/LoV/LoC
  7. Fuzzing — Sending packets that don't conform to protocol specs (invalid size, flags, gibberish payload); can be accidental (electromagnetic interference)
  8. MITM and packet injection — Intercept, alter and resend packets; packet injection sends fake packets to target device; can also cause DoS
📌 Key MCQ — Green Network Stack "Green network stacks" refers to vulnerability due to immature and poorly tested network protocols/IP stacks in ICS devices — not "interdependency with IT", not "hardware limitations". This is a frequently confused MCQ option.

Hacking Trends on the ICS

VERY CRITICAL

5.1 Consequences of Successful Cyber Incidents

Consequences range from:

  1. Relatively benign indirect disruptions
  2. Data theft (company secrets, information leaks)
  3. Alteration of operational processes / sabotage (financial loss, loss of life, shutdown)

Impacts on the Company:

  • Loss of production
  • Loss of intellectual property (information theft)
  • Replacing/fixing damaged equipment
  • Penalties for regulatory noncompliance
  • Liability claims from customers
  • Damage to business reputation

Impacts on the Public:

  • Economic impact
  • Generalized panic
  • Loss of life

Common ICS Targets:

TargetWhat attacker can do
Engineering WorkstationsExfiltrate, alter or overwrite process logic
Operator Consoles (HMI)Trick human operators into performing unintended tasks
Industrial Applications (SCADA, Historian)Manipulate process data, steal data
Network Services (Active Directory, IAM)Commonly shared between business and ICS — compromise both

5.2 Specific Attack Types in ICS

⚔️ Man-in-the-Middle (MITM) Attack ▼

Attacker goes between communicating devices, intercepts traffic and injects new traffic.

Why easy in ICS: Lack of encryption and authentication. Even when authentication is used, it's done in clear plain text.

Impact: Can manipulate data, issue commands, or monitor operations.

⚔️ Denial-of-Service (DoS) Attack ▼

Floods devices with excessive traffic. In ICS, causes LoV or LoC, which may require physical process shutdown.

Consequences of process shutdown:

  • Environmental releases (e.g., oil spills)
  • Plant shutdown
  • Mechanical failures → fire or explosion
  • Spoiled products
⚔️ Replay Attack ▼

Capture packets and retransmit them to perform a desired action. An exploitation of functionality (not a vulnerability).

Examples:

  • Enable/disable security alarms or logging
  • Transmit new programmable codes
  • Report false values
  • Perform malicious control (e.g., open circuit breaker)

Why easy in ICS: Control traffic is mostly transmitted in plain text. Commands/values can be easily modified to create custom packets.

Even with auth/encryption: Authentication messages can also be replayed to allow the attacker to authenticate.

⚔️ Compromising the HMI ▼

Exploit HMI device vulnerability to install remote access (e.g., VNC server).

Result: Attacker can directly monitor and control whatever the console is responsible for.

⚔️ Compromising the Engineering Workstation (EWS) ▼

EWS is especially dangerous because it can:

  1. Configure role-based access control
  2. Use specialized tools to directly communicate with and configure primary control equipment (PLCs, IEDs, SIS)
  3. Contains sensitive documentation (ICS design, configuration, plant operation)
📌 Section C 2526 Exam The Section C scenario-based question (20 marks) in the latest exam asks about a compromised EWS in a water treatment facility. Know the 3 things an attacker can gain from EWS: access credentials, process logic/PLC configuration, and sensitive documentation.

5.3 Functionality vs Vulnerability

Many ICS devices can be attacked via exploitation of functionality vs exploitation of vulnerability.

Example: Issuing a "shutdown" command to a device does not represent any weakness in the device. However, the lack of authentication that enables a malicious user to inject a shutdown command IS a major vulnerability.

→ Replay attacks and some DoS attacks exploit functionality, not vulnerabilities.

Attack vs Compromise:

5.4 Attack Trends

  • "Up the stack" trend: Moving from link-layer/network-layer vulnerabilities → application-specific exploits
  • Client-side application shift: Web browsers, mobile apps (run on all platforms, easily exploited via chat/social media)
  • Industrial application-layer attacks: Target SCADA, PLC, RTU communications directly
  • AI-assisted threats: Deepfakes, AI-assisted spoofing, AI-written malware
📌 MCQ — "Up the Stack" "Up the stack trends means threats are focusing more towards application-specific exploits." Not "threats from OT to IT" or "threats from physical stacks".

5.5 Dealing with an Infection — 3-Step Process

⚠️ Key Principle Upon detecting an infection, do NOT immediately clean the system! There may be dormant subsequent infections that get activated, and valuable forensic information may be lost.

Step 1: Isolate (don't clean yet)

Logically isolate the infected host. Allow malware to communicate on C&C channel (to gather intelligence) BUT isolate it from the rest of the network and remove access to sensitive information.

Step 2: Investigate / Collect Forensics

  • Collect system logs, captured network traffic, memory analysis
  • Clone disk images to preserve original state for offline analysis
  • Use sandboxing technology to analyze malware behavior
  • Reverse-engineer the malware to determine capabilities, attack vectors, propagation
  • Retain all information for disclosure to authorities

Step 3: Recover — Bare Metal Reload

Bare metal reload: Device is completely erased and reduced to a bare, inoperable state. Hardware must be reimaged completely (erase and reinstall all firmware).

Prerequisite: Clean versions of OS, applications, and asset firmware must be kept in a safe, clean environment (secure virtual backup or secured removable media in a locked cabinet at a separate physical location).

📌 MCQ — "Bare Metal Reload" "Bare metal reload means an infected device is completely erased and reset as new." NOT "a reboot to recover forensic evidence."

6.2 Risk Assessment Methodology — 6 Steps

  2. Trust boundaries and entry points (including hidden ones like built-in WiFi/Bluetooth)

  • Control measures
  • Countermeasures
  • Improvements

7.1 Defense in Depth — Definition

Defense in Depth: The coordinated use of multiple security countermeasures to protect assets.

It is a layered approach where each layer provides protection — even if one layer fails, the next layer provides protection. In ICS, all layers must be enforced; one weak layer makes the whole system vulnerable.

7.2 The 7 Layers of Defense in Depth (Stuxnet Case Study)

LayerNameWhat Should Be DoneWhat Failed in Stuxnet
1 (Outer)Policies, Procedures, AwarenessStrict USB/removable media policy; security awareness for engineers; vendor/contractor access control; ICS-specific cybersecurity governance; change management policy for PLC logicEngineers used infected USB drives; overconfidence in "air-gap"; lack of awareness of cyber-physical attacks
2Physical SecurityRestricted access to EWS; USB port control/locking; secure control room access; device whitelisting (approved hardware only)USB devices were physically inserted; no restriction on portable media
3Perimeter SecurityStrong IT/OT network separation; firewalls between zones; DMZ for data exchange; strict inbound/outbound filteringAir-gap assumed to be enough; no layered perimeter enforcement
4Internal Network SecurityNetwork segmentation (zones & conduits); east-west traffic monitoring; ICS-aware IDS/IPS; strict communication rulesMalware spread laterally; no internal monitoring
5Host SecurityPatch management (Windows); application whitelisting; EDR (Endpoint Detection & Response); least privilege access; device hardeningMultiple zero-day exploits succeeded; no strong endpoint protection
6Application SecuritySecure PLC programming environment; code signing for control logic; integrity verification; secure SCADA configurationStep7 software trusted blindly; malicious code injected into PLC logic
7 (Core)Data SecurityIntegrity monitoring of PLC data; anomaly detection (e.g., abnormal centrifuge speed); independent sensor validation; logging and alertingOperators saw false normal readings; data was manipulated (not just stolen)
📌 Exam Discussion Question (appears in latest paper) "Which layer would have stopped Stuxnet earliest?" → Layer 1 (Policies) — if strict USB policies were enforced, Stuxnet could never have entered. OR Layer 2 (Physical Security) — if USB ports were locked, physical insertion was impossible.

"Which layer is most ignored in ICS?" → Layer 1 (Policies) and Layer 7 (Data integrity) are most commonly overlooked.

"Why is data integrity more important than confidentiality in ICS?" → Because ICS controls physical processes. Manipulated data (MoV/MoC) can cause physical destruction. In IT, confidentiality is most important; in ICS, availability and integrity are more critical.

"Is air-gap a perimeter control or a misconception?" → It is largely a misconception because in reality, air gaps rarely exist — they are bypassed by USB drives, remote access, and network convergence.

7.3 Zones and Conduits

Zone: A logical grouping of assets that share similar security requirements. Assets within the same zone can communicate freely with each other.

Conduit: A communication flow arrangement that groups communications into a logical flow of information within and between various security zones. Controls what can pass between zones.

Q1: ICS Definition, Tasks & Components [10 marks] ▼

(a) Define ICS [1 mark]: An Industrial Control System (ICS) is a broad class of automation systems used for control and monitoring functionality in manufacturing and industrial facilities.

(b) THREE general tasks of ICS [3 marks]:

  1. Collect data from endpoint devices
  2. Send collected data to an HMI (Human-Machine Interface) to be displayed
  3. Apply automatic, semi-automatic or operator-controlled changes to endpoint devices

(c) THREE components of ICS [6 marks — 2 each]:

PLC (Programmable Logic Controller): A specialized industrial computer that can be programmed to automate functions within manufacturing facilities. It executes control logic based on sensor inputs and controls actuators/outputs accordingly.

RTU (Remote Terminal Unit): Collects data from field devices and transmits the data back to a central monitoring station. May also receive control signals from a process control computer and relay them to field devices. Requires communication capability for data transmission.

HMI (Human-Machine Interface): The device used by human operators to view data collected from field devices. May also enable human operators to control certain devices. Can be touch-based screens, computer terminals, push buttons, auditory feedback, or graphical displays providing topological diagrams, charts, and dials.

Q2: Threats — Direct/Indirect, Motivations, Policy [10 marks] ▼

(a) Direct vs Indirect Threats [4 marks]:

See Ch3 section 3.3 for full answer. Key: Direct = intentional, attacker controls ICS. Indirect = unintentional, collateral damage from scanning/probing.

(b) TWO motivations for ICS attacks [2 marks]:

  1. Financial gain (ransomware, data theft, credential theft)
  2. Sabotage — to cause physical damage, equipment malfunction, injuries, utility outages

(c) Policy to safeguard ICS from outsider threats [4 marks]:

Sample policy with justification:

  1. Network Segmentation Policy: Mandatory separation between IT business network and OT/ICS network using firewalls and DMZ. Justification: prevents malware from traveling from compromised business systems to ICS.
  2. Remote Access Policy: All remote access must use VPN with multi-factor authentication. Only approved vendor accounts allowed with time-limited, monitored sessions. Justification: attackers exploit weak remote access credentials (as seen in Colonial Pipeline attack).
  3. Removable Media Policy: All USB and removable media strictly prohibited unless formally authorized and scanned. Justification: USBs are the primary vector for bypassing air gaps (Stuxnet).
  4. Intrusion Detection Policy: Deploy ICS-aware IDS/IPS to monitor industrial protocol traffic for anomalies. Justification: detect intrusions early before they reach critical ICS assets.
Q3: Vulnerabilities — Management, Guides, Functional Impact [10 marks] ▼

(a) TWO ICS management vulnerabilities [4 marks]:

1. Lack of ERM (Enterprise Risk Management) [2 marks]: ERM specifies requirements and guidelines for managing security risks and links ICS security to high-level corporate goals. Without ERM, resources for ICS controls are difficult to obtain, and there is no connection between security activities and organizational objectives.

2. Lack of policies defining roles and responsibilities [2 marks]: Without clear policies, ownership of ICS security is poorly defined. Without clear responsibility, there is no accountability. This means nobody is specifically responsible when a security breach occurs.

(b) TWO guides for better ICS security [2 marks]:

  1. NIST 800-82 — Guide to Industrial Control System Security (a widely referenced standard)
  2. Use defense-in-depth strategies — multiple layers of security countermeasures

(c) TWO types of functional vulnerability impacts on CONTROL interface [4 marks]:

Denial of Control (DoC) [2 marks]: Temporary inability to control an ICS asset. Can be intentional or unintentional. Safeguard: redundant control paths, fault-tolerant design, backup manual controls.

Manipulation of Control (MoC) [2 marks]: Attacker overrides or intercepts and changes operator commands, applying inappropriate control sequences. May cause catastrophic reactions. Safeguard: integrity checking of control commands, mutual authentication between operators and devices, anomaly detection on control commands.

Q4: Industrial Network, Vulnerabilities, Attacks & Countermeasures [10 marks] ▼

(a) Industrial network example + common vulnerability [2 marks]:

Example: SCADA network (used in water treatment, power grid). Common vulnerability: Lack of authentication in communication protocols between SCADA master and RTUs — any device can issue commands without proving its identity.

(b) TWO attack types exploiting this vulnerability [4 marks]:

1. MITM Attack: Attacker positions between SCADA master and RTU. Intercepts commands, alters them (e.g., changes valve position command), and forwards modified commands. Because there is no authentication, the RTU accepts the modified command as legitimate.

2. Replay Attack: Attacker captures valid command packets (e.g., "open valve" packet) and retransmits them repeatedly. Because the protocol lacks authentication timestamps, the RTU accepts and executes the replayed commands, potentially causing uncontrolled valve operations.

(c) TWO security countermeasures [4 marks]:

1. Authentication and Encryption: Implement mutual authentication between SCADA master and field devices. Use encrypted channels (e.g., TLS) to prevent eavesdropping. This prevents MITM and replay attacks as commands are authenticated and cannot be replayed.

2. Network Segmentation + IDS: Deploy ICS-aware IDS that monitors for anomalous traffic patterns (unusual command frequency, unusual sources). Segment the network so unauthorized devices cannot reach the SCADA master.

Q7: APT Malware Scenario (uSBRootKit) + Defense in Depth [20 marks] ▼

Scenario: uSBRootKit enters via USB, infects machines, seeks admin credentials, targets historical data, publishes IP/MAC addresses on backup transmissions. Your SCADA is air-gapped with one-way air gap network.

(a) TWO methods for malware to enter [4 marks]:

  1. Infected USB drive: A trusted insider (maintenance contractor, engineer) unknowingly inserts an infected USB drive into an ICS workstation. Even in an air-gapped network, USB is a physical medium that bypasses network security controls. This is exactly how Stuxnet spread.
  2. One-way air gap network: Although described as one-way, if the one-way air gap device (data diode) can receive data, there may be covert channels. Alternatively, if maintenance laptops or backup media are shared between the air-gapped network and external networks, they could carry the malware.

(b) TWO mitigation plans [4 marks]:

  1. Strict removable media policy: All USB drives must be formally approved, scanned for malware, and logged before use. Consider disabling USB ports physically or through software (USB port blockers). All portable media used in the ICS must ONLY be used within the ICS environment.
  2. Application whitelisting: Implement software whitelisting so that only pre-approved applications can run on ICS workstations. The uSBRootKit, when executed, would be blocked because it is not on the approved list. This prevents unauthorized software from running even if a USB is inserted.

(c) TWO actions to detect infection [4 marks]:

  1. Monitoring backup transmissions: Since the malware activates when backup transmission occurs, set up anomaly detection specifically on backup transmission activities. Look for unexpected outbound connections or unusual data volumes during backup periods.
  2. Network traffic analysis: Monitor the ICS network for unexpected IP/MAC address lookups or anomalous traffic patterns. Use network flow analysis to detect unauthorized communications that should not occur in an air-gapped network.

(d) Defense-in-Depth document [8 marks]:

Apply the 7 layers:

  • Layer 1 (Policy): Strict USB policy (no unauthorized media), staff security awareness training on APT threats, incident response plan for USB-based malware.
  • Layer 2 (Physical): Lock/disable all USB ports. Physical access control to engineering workstations — only authorized personnel. CCTV monitoring of control room.
  • Layer 3 (Perimeter): Maintain strict air gap; verify integrity of one-way connection device. Strictly control all data entering the ICS environment.
  • Layer 4 (Internal Network): Network segmentation between historian server and other ICS assets. Monitoring of lateral movement within the ICS network.
  • Layer 5 (Host): Application whitelisting on all hosts. Endpoint protection software. Disable autorun features on all ICS computers.
  • Layer 6 (Application): Integrity monitoring of historian application. Alert on unexpected data access patterns. Secure the backup transmission application.
  • Layer 7 (Data): Encrypt backup data. Monitor and alert on any attempt to publish or exfiltrate IP/MAC address information. Data loss prevention (DLP) tools.

Q3: Threat vs Vulnerability, IT vs OT, Data States [10 marks] ▼

(a) Difference between threat and vulnerability for ICS [2 marks]:

Threat: A possible danger that might exploit a vulnerability to breach security and cause harm. It is the potential source of risk (e.g., a nation-state actor, malware, a phishing email).

Vulnerability: A weakness in the ICS that can be exploited by a threat. It is the flaw or gap in security (e.g., lack of authentication in Modbus protocol, unpatched Windows vulnerability).

Relationship: A threat exploits a vulnerability to cause harm. Without a vulnerability, a threat cannot cause harm.

IT: Client-server communication pattern. Communication is straightforward and easy to monitor. Servers can be located at different locations. Encryption is standard.

OT/ICS: A mixture of peer-to-peer AND client-server communication. A single process instruction may cause multiple communications between multiple devices, making traffic inspection difficult. Encryption introduces extra cost and latency (rare in OT). Network implementation is more complex and harder to inspect.

Q5: Risk Assessment — Definition, Purpose, Assessment vs Testing [10 marks] ▼

(c) Difference between risk assessment and security testing [2 marks]: Risk assessment identifies what controls should be implemented based on presumed risk (theoretical analysis). Q1: IT vs ICS Failures, IT Security Unsuitability, Data in Use [10 marks] ▼

(a) IT failure vs ICS failure consequences [2 marks]:

IT failure: Primarily impacts data. Consequences include loss of data availability or confidentiality, which disrupts business operations such as transactions, reporting, or communications. Recovery is typically done by rebooting systems or restoring backups.

ICS failure: Has direct physical consequences. A failure in ICS can cause equipment damage, environmental releases, production shutdown, and most critically, injury or loss of life. Symptoms may look like ordinary maintenance failures, making them difficult to diagnose. Recovery may require physical intervention.

(b) TWO reasons IT security practices cause delays/unsuitability for ICS [4 marks]:

1. Patching/upgrading difficulty: IT security assumes that systems can be regularly patched or upgraded with minimal disruption. In ICS, old systems often cannot be patched because there are no patches available from vendors (legacy systems), or because applying patches requires system downtime — which is unacceptable in ICS where continuous availability is critical (e.g., a power plant cannot shut down for security updates).

2. Downtime unacceptable/no test environments: IT security practices include testing in development/staging environments before production deployment. In ICS, it is often not possible to have separate development and test environments because the equipment is specialized and expensive. Additionally, downtime for security updates may cause regulatory violations or safety incidents.

(c) "Data in Use" meaning and why limited manipulation in ICS [4 marks]:

Data in use refers to data that is present within the volatile memory of information processing devices or displayed on the screen of interface devices. This data will disappear if the device is turned off.

In ICS, data in use on devices like PLCs has little scope for manipulation or change because: ICS-specific devices (PLCs, RTUs, IEDs) are designed with very limited memory and processing power — just enough for their intended control function. They do not possess the memory or processing power that would allow data in use to be changed deliberately by external software. The data simply passes through quickly to control physical processes rather than being stored in ways that allow modification.

Q2: Vulnerability Categories, Wireless Susceptibility, Green Network Stacks [10 marks] ▼

(a) THREE main vulnerability categories [3 marks]:

  1. Business (Management) Vulnerabilities
  2. Operational Vulnerabilities
  3. Technical Vulnerabilities

(b) How wireless ICS susceptible to eavesdropping/masquerade [4 marks]:

Wireless networks use radio frequencies that broadcast in all directions — any device within range can receive the signals. In ICS, poor wireless system design and architecture makes this especially dangerous:

Eavesdropping: If wireless communications are unencrypted (common in legacy ICS wireless devices), an attacker with a radio receiver can capture all traffic between field devices and controllers. This reveals process data, commands, sensor readings, and potentially authentication credentials. The attacker can learn the ICS topology and operation without being detected.

Masquerade (Spoofing): Without proper authentication, an attacker can impersonate a legitimate ICS device. They set up a device with the same wireless address/frequency as a legitimate field device. Commands from the controller go to both the real device and the attacker's device. The attacker can then replay or modify responses, causing the controller to receive falsified sensor data (leading to MoV) or send unauthorized commands to the real field device.

(c) "Green network stacks" as technical attack vector [3 marks]:

Green network stacks refers to immature and poorly tested network protocols and IP stacks that appear in ICS devices. This vulnerability arises because: (1) ICS vendors rushed their products to adopt Ethernet and IP connectivity without thorough security testing; (2) These vendors still assumed their products would be isolated from business networks and the Internet, so security was not a priority; (3) The resulting network implementations contain bugs, buffer overflow vulnerabilities, and protocol implementation flaws that can be exploited by sending malformed packets (fuzzing) or specific crafted packets that cause crashes or unexpected behavior in the ICS device.

Q3: Two Communication Interfaces, LoV vs MoV [10 marks] ▼

(a) TWO ICS device communication interfaces and vulnerabilities [4 marks]:

1. IP Communication Interface (2 marks): The interface through which operators issue commands to the device via IP network. Vulnerabilities here cause: Denial of View (DoV), Loss of View (LoV), or Manipulation of View (MoV) — i.e., problems with what the operator can SEE about system state.

2. I/O Control Interface (2 marks): The interface physically connected to field devices (sensors, valves, drives) via analog communication. Controls the actual physical functions of the production infrastructure. Vulnerabilities here cause: Denial of Control (DoC), Loss of Control (LoC), or Manipulation of Control (MoC) — i.e., problems with the ability to CONTROL physical processes.

(b) LoV vs MoV differentiation and resulting risks [6 marks]:

Loss of View (LoV):

  • Cause: A sustained or permanent failure of the IP communication interface
  • What happens: Operator loses all visibility — cannot see any data from the ICS
  • IP interface: Completely down/failed
  • PLC/ICS operation: Control logic may still continue to function normally
  • Recovery: Requires local, hands-on operator intervention to fix the failure
  • Risk: Operator has no information about system state, may make inappropriate decisions or trigger emergency shutdowns unnecessarily

Manipulation of View (MoV):

  • Cause: Attacker forges and sends false ICS data to operators
  • What happens: Operator sees data but it is deliberately falsified — may show "everything normal" while system is actually failing
  • IP interface: Still functioning — data still flows, just falsified
  • PLC/ICS operation: Not directly impacted — physical control continues
  • Recovery: Difficult — operators don't know they are being deceived
  • Risk: Operators take inappropriate actions based on false information; can hide ongoing malicious activity while damage occurs; more dangerous than LoV because operators believe they are in control

Key difference: In LoV, operators know they have no information (can take precautionary measures). In MoV, operators don't know their information is wrong (they act confidently on false data — more dangerous).

Q4: Direct/Indirect Threats, Insiders vs Outsiders, Malware Hiding [10 marks] ▼

(a) Direct vs Indirect Threat with examples [4 marks]:

See Ch3 section 3.3 for full answer.

(b) Insiders vs Outsiders comparison [3 marks]:

See Ch3 section 3.5 for full comparison. Key: Insiders already have trusted access and don't need to compromise the perimeter. Outsiders must first bypass the security perimeter. ICS prefers white-box testing because insider threats are greater.

(c) THREE malware tactics to remain hidden [3 marks]:

  1. Deactivating/circumventing anti-malware: Once installed, malware disables antivirus software and IDS so they cannot detect or remove it. The malware operates in the now-unprotected environment without detection.
  2. Installing persistent rootkits: Rootkits modify the operating system at a low level to hide the malware's presence from the OS itself. Even if the system is scanned, the rootkit makes the malware invisible by intercepting system calls that would reveal its files or processes.
  3. Deleting trace files / hiding in file system: Malware removes its installation footprint by deleting log entries, temporary files, and installer files. It may also set its files as system, hidden, and read-only to prevent them from appearing in normal directory listings. Some malware reverts to a packed/compressed form after installation to evade signature-based detection.
Q5: ERM, Poor Security Practices, Vulnerability Testing [10 marks] ▼

(a) Critical management vulnerability: Lacking ERM [3 marks]:

Enterprise Risk Management (ERM) specifies requirements and guidelines for managing security risks and links the security of ICS to high-level corporate goals and objectives. Without ERM: (1) Resources for ICS security controls are difficult to obtain because there is no formal justification linking ICS security investment to business outcomes; (2) Security activities are disconnected from business strategy — ICS security becomes an afterthought; (3) ICS security becomes ad hoc — done only when there is an incident rather than proactively as an ongoing program. This creates systematic under-investment in ICS security.

(b) TWO poor security practices from administrative efficiency [4 marks]:

1. Sharing administrator accounts [2 marks]: In ICS environments, administrative efficiency encourages sharing a single administrator account among multiple operators (to simplify management). This means: if a security incident occurs, it is impossible to determine which specific individual performed a given action (no non-repudiation). Forensic investigation is severely hampered because audit logs show the shared account but cannot identify the individual. Accountability is lost.

2. Complete overlap of administrative duties / common passwords [2 marks]: When all administrators have the same role with no separation of duties, and use the same password, a single compromised credential gives an attacker full administrative access to the entire ICS. There is no "least privilege" — every admin can do everything. This makes it impossible to contain the damage when a credential is compromised, and impossible to attribute actions to specific individuals in forensic analysis.

(c) Importance of Vulnerability Testing in acceptance testing [3 marks]:

Acceptance testing verifies that new, upgraded, patched or changed assets perform as expected functionally. However, Vulnerability Testing specifically ensures that no new vulnerabilities have been introduced as a result of the change. For example: a patch may close one vulnerability but inadvertently open a new port or service, creating a new attack surface. Without vulnerability testing, this new vulnerability goes undetected in production. In ICS, where changes are infrequent but high-stakes, missing a newly introduced vulnerability can have severe consequences as these systems operate for 15–20 years before replacement. Vulnerability testing provides assurance that the security posture has not degraded after any change.

Q6: Defense in Depth, Zones & Conduits, Pen Test vs Security Audit [10 marks] ▼

(a) Define Defense in Depth [2 marks]: Defense in Depth is defined as the coordinated use of multiple security countermeasures to protect assets. It is a layered security strategy where each layer provides protection, so that if one layer is breached, subsequent layers continue to provide defense. No single security measure is relied upon to protect the entire ICS.

(b) Zones and Conduits + which scenario poses greater risk [4 marks]:

Zones: Logical groupings of ICS assets that share similar security requirements. Assets within the same zone can communicate according to defined rules. Zones limit the blast radius of a compromise — if one zone is compromised, proper conduit controls prevent attackers from moving freely to other zones.

Conduits: Communication flow arrangements that group communications between security zones. A conduit controls exactly what traffic is permitted to flow between zones, through mechanisms like firewalls, protocol inspection, and authentication. A conduit can also be a physical connection (a cable) or logical connection (a VPN tunnel) with security controls at each endpoint.

Greater risk scenario — Remote vendor access (VPN) to ICS: The scenario where a vendor has direct remote access to the control zone poses the greatest risk. If the VPN credentials are compromised (weak passwords, no MFA), an attacker gains a path directly into the ICS control zone. There is no physical barrier. Mitigation: Place vendor remote access in a separate DMZ zone with a tightly controlled conduit (allowing only specific protocol and specific device access, with full session logging, and time-limited connections).

(c) Pen Testing benefit over Security Audit, despite system impact [4 marks]:

Security Audit tests against predefined policies, procedures, and standards. It checks whether controls exist on paper and whether compliance is met. However, it cannot reveal unknown vulnerabilities — it only finds what it knows to look for.

Penetration Testing provides a REAL proof of exploitability. Even if a vulnerability exists in theory, management may not prioritize fixing it without evidence that it can actually be exploited. A pen test provides this proof — "here is the screenshot showing we accessed your PLC without authorization."

The primary benefit: Penetration testing provides an empirical, evidence-based demonstration that a vulnerability can be exploited under real-world conditions. This is significantly more persuasive to senior management than a theoretical audit finding. The risk of impact to the ICS is accepted because the benefit of this proof often results in faster, better-funded security improvements that would not occur otherwise.

Section C — Water Treatment Facility Scenario [20 marks]

⚠️ NEW Section — 20 Marks This section did not exist in earlier papers. It is a complex scenario that requires integrating knowledge from multiple chapters. Study this carefully as it is likely to appear in your exam.

Scenario: Water treatment facility. External vendor has remote access (VPN) to PLC and HMI systems. Attacker exploits weak VPN credentials, compromises EWS on industrial network. Goal: catastrophic Loss of Control (LoC) in water pumps.

Q1(a): THREE capabilities gained from compromised EWS [6 marks] ▼
📝 Full Answer (2 marks each)

1. PLC Programming Logic and Configuration Access (2 marks): The EWS contains specialized tools (e.g., Siemens Step7, Rockwell Studio 5000) that are used to directly program and configure PLCs. By accessing the EWS, the attacker can review the existing PLC ladder logic to understand exactly how the water pump control system operates — what sensor thresholds trigger pump activation, what safety limits exist, and what commands control pump speed. This intelligence is essential for crafting a MoC attack that causes LoC without triggering obvious alarms.

2. Role-Based Access Control Configuration (2 marks): The EWS has the capability to configure role-based access control for the ICS. The attacker can use this to: create new administrative accounts for persistent access; modify existing account permissions to elevate privileges; or disable access controls for other operators so that legitimate operators are locked out (contributing to LoC). This gives the attacker sustained, privileged access even after the initial vector is discovered and blocked.

3. Sensitive ICS Documentation and Network Topology (2 marks): The EWS contains sensitive documentation about the ICS design, configuration, and plant operation — including network diagrams, device IP addresses, safety system configurations, and operational procedures. This information allows the attacker to identify all connected PLCs and their functions, plan which devices to target for maximum impact (e.g., the specific PLC controlling water distribution pumps), and understand safety systems that might interrupt the attack.

Q1(b): How MoC achieves catastrophic LoC without destroying hardware [4 marks] ▼
📝 Full Answer

Manipulation of Control (MoC) involves overriding or intercepting and changing legitimate operator commands, applying inappropriate control sequences that introduce defects and catastrophic reactions within the production process. The I/O control interface and IP communication interface remain physically intact.

How the attacker achieves LoC through MoC in the water facility:

The attacker, using the PLC programming access gained from the EWS, reprograms the PLC logic for the water pumps. They insert malicious logic that: (1) Responds to normal operator commands with seemingly normal acknowledgment signals (the operator sees the command was accepted); (2) But executes a different action internally — for example, running pumps at dangerous speeds, closing/opening valves in dangerous sequences, or disabling pump safety interlocks; (3) Simultaneously sends falsified status data back to the HMI showing that the pumps are operating normally (combining MoC with MoV).

When operators attempt to regain control by issuing shutdown or adjustment commands, the malicious PLC logic overrides them or acknowledges them without executing them. Eventually, the pump system reaches a state where: operators cannot override the PLC behavior (LoC is achieved), the system may experience physical damage from abnormal operation (cavitation, overheating, over-pressurization), or the water treatment process fails, affecting public water safety.

This achieves catastrophic LoC without destroying hardware because the hardware (pumps, PLCs) are intact — it is the control logic that has been subverted, rendering hardware control impossible through normal means.

Q1(c): Defense in Depth Strategy with Zones & Conduits [10 marks] ▼
📝 Full Answer — Structure Your Answer as a Strategy Document

Zone Architecture (4 marks):

Zone 1 — Enterprise Zone: Business systems, corporate email, finance systems. No direct connection to ICS.

Zone 2 — DMZ (Demilitarized Zone): Data exchange buffer between enterprise and ICS. Historian server for data access. Vendor remote access gateway. Email relay for alerts.

Zone 3 — Supervisory Zone: SCADA servers, HMI servers, data concentration. Limited access — only from DMZ via controlled conduit.

Zone 4 — Control Zone: Engineering Workstations (EWS), PLCs, RTUs, field devices. Highest security. No direct internet access. Access only from Supervisory Zone via strict conduit.

Zone 5 — Remote Vendor Access Zone: Isolated network segment specifically for vendor remote access. Strictly limited connectivity — only to specific assets the vendor needs to access.

Conduit Controls — Remote Access Conduit (mitigating VPN vulnerability) [4 marks]:

The remote access conduit connects Vendor Zone → DMZ → Supervisory Zone (never directly to Control Zone):

  • Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) on all VPN accounts — compromised password alone is insufficient
  • Privileged Access Workstation (PAW) — vendor must use a dedicated, locked-down device for ICS access
  • Just-In-Time (JIT) access — vendor accounts are activated only for the duration of the maintenance window, deactivated after
  • Session recording and monitoring — all vendor sessions are recorded and monitored in real-time by security staff
  • Protocol inspection at conduit — only specific ICS protocols (e.g., RDP to specific HMI, Step7 to specific PLC) allowed; all other traffic blocked
  • Network segregation — vendor cannot access the EWS directly; they access only a jump server in the DMZ that has limited connectivity to specific ICS assets they need

Containing EWS compromise [2 marks]:

If EWS is compromised despite all controls: EWS is isolated in a micro-segment within the Control Zone. Outbound connections from EWS are strictly controlled — it cannot initiate new connections (only respond to authenticated polling). PLC logic integrity monitoring — any change to PLC programming triggers an alert and requires dual-approval. EWS activity logs are forwarded in real-time to a Security Operations Center (SOC) outside the ICS network, so even if logs on the EWS are tampered with, the SOC retains copies.

VulnerabilityA weakness in a system that can be exploited by a threat to cause harm.
RiskThe potential that a given threat will exploit vulnerabilities of an asset and thereby cause harm to the organization.
APTAdvanced Persistent Threat — malware that is difficult to detect and can continue operating even if detected and removed or the system is rebooted.
Weaponized MalwareMalware that exhibits a certain degree of sophistication and shows a clear motive and intent (e.g., targeted sabotage).
Security Through ObscurityThe belief that keeping a protocol/system secret provides security because nobody knows how to exploit it. NOT effective in modern day.
Defense in DepthThe coordinated use of multiple security countermeasures to protect assets; a layered security approach.
ZoneA logical grouping of assets that share similar security requirements in an ICS network.
ConduitA communication flow arrangement that groups communications into a logical flow between security zones; controls traffic between zones.
CVSSCommon Vulnerability Scoring System — a tool for ranking and prioritizing vulnerabilities by severity.
Bare Metal ReloadComplete erasure and reinstallation of a device to a bare inoperable state; removes all firmware and software including malware.
PivotingMalware propagating from one infected system to other systems in the network to reach the intended target.
BYODBring Your Own Device — policy allowing employees to use personal devices for work; introduces security risks.
EWSEngineering Workstation — has specialized tools to configure and program PLCs, IEDs, SIS; contains sensitive ICS documentation.
MoVManipulation of View — attacker sends forged data to operators, who see false system state without impact on the communication interface itself.
MoCManipulation of Control — attacker overrides operator commands with inappropriate control sequences, potentially causing catastrophic results.
LoCLoss of Control — sustained inability for operators to issue commands to ICS assets; requires local intervention to restore.
ITInformation Technology — computing for information processing (software, hardware, communications). CIA priority: Confidentiality first.
OTOperational Technology — computing for industrial operations, monitoring/control of physical devices. CIA priority: Availability first.
Green Network StacksImmature and poorly tested network protocols and IP stacks in ICS devices; created security vulnerabilities when vendors rushed products to IP market.
White Box TestingSecurity testing where the tester has complete information about the ICS (topology, assets, configuration). Reflects insider knowledge. Preferred for ICS.
Black Box TestingSecurity testing with minimal information (e.g., only IP address range). Reflects outsider knowledge.
